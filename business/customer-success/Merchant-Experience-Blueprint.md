## 1. Executive Summary

### Purpose of This Blueprint

This document maps every significant interaction a merchant has with OneMember — from the moment they first hear about the product to the moment they recommend it to another business owner. It is a working reference, not a theoretical model. Every team that touches the merchant — product, design, marketing, sales, support, and customer success — should use this document to understand where the merchant is in their journey and what they need at that exact moment.

The blueprint is built around the Ideal Customer Profile defined in the Merchant Personas document: an owner-operated small business in Thailand with 2–50 employees, currently using paper stamp cards or no loyalty system, serving repeat customers in food, beauty, wellness, or retail. Everything described here is calibrated to that merchant.

### Why Merchant Experience Matters

OneMember's business model depends on a single chain of events: a merchant discovers the product, starts a free trial, experiences real value before the trial ends, and subscribes. If any link in that chain breaks — if the product is confusing at sign-up, if the first two weeks produce no visible result, if the conversion moment is poorly timed — the merchant is lost and the acquisition cost is wasted.

In a subscription SaaS business targeting small merchants, the cost of poor experience compounds. Merchants talk to each other. A frustrated merchant who cancels tells people. A delighted merchant who succeeds tells people. The quality of the experience is not just a product concern — it is the primary marketing asset.

OneMember is not competing on feature count. It is competing on how simple and effective the experience feels to a person who is not a technology expert and is evaluating the product between serving customers. Every friction point is a competitor's advantage.

### Definition of Success

A successful merchant experience is one where:

1. The merchant understands what OneMember does within two minutes of seeing it for the first time.
2. The merchant completes onboarding on the same day they sign up.
3. The merchant adds their first real customer within 48 hours of completing onboarding.
4. The merchant sees their first reward redemption within 30 days of signing up.
5. The merchant subscribes before or at trial expiry because they cannot imagine running without it.
6. The merchant mentions OneMember to at least one other business owner in their first three months.

Every section of this blueprint contributes to one or more of those six outcomes.

## 2. Merchant Journey Overview

The merchant journey consists of ten stages. Each stage has a distinct merchant objective, a characteristic mindset, and a specific outcome OneMember needs to deliver.

```
AWARENESS → INTEREST → EVALUATION → TRIAL → ONBOARDING
    → ACTIVATION → VALUE REALISATION → SUBSCRIPTION
        → EXPANSION → ADVOCACY
```

| Stage | Merchant Objective | Merchant Mindset | Desired OneMember Outcome |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Awareness** | Understand that a problem exists and that solutions exist | "I know I should be doing something about this." | Merchant encounters OneMember and immediately understands what it does |
| **Interest** | Learn whether OneMember is relevant to their specific business | "This might be for me — let me find out more." | Merchant sees themselves and their business type reflected in the messaging |
| **Evaluation** | Decide whether to try OneMember | "Is this worth my time? Will this work for my business?" | Merchant reaches the sign-up page with confidence and without unanswered questions |
| **Trial** | Register and access the product | "I'm in. Let me see what this looks like." | Merchant completes registration and lands in a product that feels immediately welcoming |
| **Onboarding** | Set up the business and create a loyalty programme | "I need to get this ready before I open tomorrow." | Merchant completes the wizard and has a working loyalty programme the same day |
| **Activation** | Add real customers and record real transactions | "I want to see this actually work." | Merchant adds their first real member and records their first real purchase |
| **Value Realisation** | Witness the loyalty cycle in action | "A customer just came back because of the reward. This works." | Merchant experiences a tangible event that proves the product delivers its promise |
| **Subscription** | Decide to pay for OneMember | "I don't want to lose what I've built. This is worth it." | Merchant converts from trial to paid plan before or at trial expiry |
| **Expansion** | Grow the programme and increase reliance on OneMember | "I want to get more out of this." | Merchant deepens product usage, grows member base, explores new campaign types |
| **Advocacy** | Recommend OneMember to other businesses | "I've been telling everyone about this." | Merchant actively refers at least one other business to OneMember |

## 3. Journey Stage Details

---

### Stage 1 — Awareness

**Merchant Goal:** Recognise that a problem exists — specifically, that customers are not returning as often as they should — and encounter OneMember as a possible answer.

**Merchant Thoughts:**
- "We get new customers but most of them never come back."
- "I know I should have a loyalty programme but it sounds complicated."
- "Paper cards aren't working — half my customers lose them."
- "My competitor just started something with a loyalty app. I need to look into this."

**Merchant Questions:**
- "What is this? Is this relevant to a business like mine?"
- "How is this different from what I've heard about before?"
- "Is this going to be expensive or complicated?"

**Merchant Emotions:** Mildly dissatisfied. Curious but cautious. The awareness moment is rarely dramatic — it is usually a low-grade recognition that something is missing combined with an external trigger.

**Merchant Pain Points:**
- Cannot articulate exactly what they want — they know the problem (customers not returning) but not the solution category
- Limited time to research — any marketing content needs to communicate in seconds, not minutes
- Scepticism about technology — has seen products that promised simplicity and delivered complexity

**Merchant Risks:**
- Encounters the brand but does not understand what it does — moves on
- Encounters the brand but assumes it is too complicated or too expensive before investigating
- Encounters the brand but the timing is not right — files it away and forgets

**What OneMember Should Communicate:**
The problem first, not the product. "Your customers already visited once. OneMember helps bring them back." Merchants do not search for loyalty software — they search for solutions to the problem of customers not returning. Lead with the outcome.

**What the UI Should Achieve:**
At the Awareness stage, the merchant is not inside the product. The "UI" is the website, the ad, the social post. It must communicate the core value proposition in the first sentence. It must not require scrolling to understand what OneMember does or who it is for.

**What Support Should Do:**
No direct support role at this stage. Support content (help articles, FAQs) should be SEO-optimised so that merchants searching for "how to build customer loyalty" or "replace paper stamp card" find relevant, useful content before reaching the product.

**What Marketing Should Do:**
Lead with problem-first messaging at all times. Use industry-specific language so the merchant immediately recognises their own situation. "For coffee shops" resonates more than "for small businesses." Test messaging across the priority industries identified in the Merchant Personas scorecard.

**What Success Looks Like:**
The merchant clicks through to the website or landing page having understood — before arriving — that OneMember helps businesses keep customers coming back and that it is built for businesses like theirs.

**KPIs:**
- Click-through rate on paid ads
- Organic search impressions and rankings for problem-first keywords
- Website traffic from target industry segments
- Bounce rate on the homepage (high bounce = message not landing)

**Opportunities for Automation:**
Retargeting campaigns that follow merchants who viewed the website without signing up. Industry-specific ad sequences that deepen the message for merchants who clicked once but did not convert.

**Possible Future AI Assistance:** [V2 IDEA] Personalised website experience that detects the referring source (ad for "café loyalty" vs "salon rewards") and adjusts the homepage headline and social proof accordingly.

### Stage 2 — Interest

**Merchant Goal:** Confirm that OneMember is relevant to their specific business type and understand what it would actually look like in practice.

**Merchant Thoughts:**
- "Okay, this might be what I'm looking for. Let me read more."
- "Is this for businesses like mine, or is it for bigger companies?"
- "What does it actually do, step by step?"
- "How much does it cost?"

**Merchant Questions:**
- "Does this work for a hair salon / café / gym / restaurant?" (specific to their industry)
- "Do my customers need to download an app?"
- "How long does it take to set up?"
- "Is there a free trial?"

**Merchant Emotions:** Hopeful but still cautious. The merchant is mentally rehearsing whether this could work for them — imagining a customer at their counter, staff using a new system, the first reward being issued.

**Merchant Pain Points:**
- Generic SaaS language that could apply to any industry does not reassure a café owner that this was built for them
- Pricing visibility — if pricing is hidden until after sign-up, many merchants will not proceed
- Too much information to process quickly — feature lists are less useful than outcome descriptions

**Merchant Risks:**
- Cannot find an answer to "does this work for my business type" and abandons
- Assumes the product requires technical setup and self-eliminates
- Gets distracted by price before understanding value

**What OneMember Should Communicate:**
Industry relevance. "Used by cafés, salons, restaurants, and pet grooming businesses across Thailand." A clear, simple explanation of how a merchant uses the product in one working day. The answer to the app question before it is asked.

**What the UI Should Achieve:**
The website or landing page should make the merchant feel seen. Use their industry language. Show what the product looks like in a context they recognise. Answer the three most common pre-signup questions without requiring the merchant to search for them: How does it work? Do customers need an app? How much does it cost?

**What Support Should Do:**
Ensure the FAQ page and help content addresses the most common pre-signup questions by industry. Monitor incoming chat or email enquiries from this stage to identify gaps in the website's ability to address concerns.

**What Marketing Should Do:**
Industry-specific landing pages or sections that speak directly to each persona. A café owner should land on a page that uses café language, café examples, and a café-relevant CTA. The same product, positioned specifically.

**What Success Looks Like:**
The merchant reaches the sign-up page with their core questions answered, their industry recognised, and their expectation calibrated: this is simple, there is a free trial, and there is no app for their customers.

**KPIs:**
- Time on site per session
- Pricing page views (intent signal)
- FAQ page engagement
- Sign-up page arrival rate from interest-stage pages

**Opportunities for Automation:**
Exit-intent prompt offering a quick demo or a specific piece of content ("See how a café uses OneMember in 10 minutes").

**Possible Future AI Assistance:** [V2 IDEA] A conversational chatbot on the website that answers industry-specific questions without requiring the merchant to read through a pricing page.

### Stage 3 — Evaluation

**Merchant Goal:** Make the decision to try OneMember. Resolve any remaining doubts. Understand exactly what they are committing to.

**Merchant Thoughts:**
- "The trial is free. There's nothing to lose by trying."
- "I don't have to give my credit card. Okay, let me try it."
- "If it's as simple as they say, I'll know in 10 minutes."
- "What happens to my data if I cancel?"

**Merchant Questions:**
- "What exactly does the free trial include?"
- "What happens at the end of the trial?"
- "Do I have to pay now?"
- "Can I cancel at any time?"
- "Will my customer data be safe?"

**Merchant Emotions:** Cautiously optimistic. The free trial structure removes financial risk but the merchant is still making a mental commitment — they are about to invest time. The evaluation stage is where time becomes the primary cost, not money.

**Merchant Pain Points:**
- Any sign-up friction — too many fields, confusing form, slow loading — breaks the momentum built up through Awareness and Interest
- Uncertainty about what they are committing to — any hidden obligation creates hesitation
- The cost of being wrong — a merchant who has tried a complicated product before is twice as reluctant to try again

**Merchant Risks:**
- Abandons the sign-up form mid-way due to friction or length
- Completes sign-up but has the wrong expectation — expected to see something immediately and lands on a blank screen
- Signs up on a mobile device and the experience is not optimised — saves it for later and forgets

**What OneMember Should Communicate:**
Commitment clarity. "30 days free. No credit card. Cancel anytime." The sign-up page should be the simplest page in the entire experience. The merchant should never be uncertain about what happens next.

**What the UI Should Achieve:**
The registration form must be minimal. Name, email, password — nothing more at this stage. Business details are collected in the onboarding wizard. A progress signal immediately after registration (a welcome message, a next step) that creates forward momentum.

**What Support Should Do:**
No direct support role. Ensure the privacy policy and data handling information is accessible from the sign-up page — not because most merchants will read it, but because the visible presence of it communicates trustworthiness.

**What Marketing Should Do:**
Reinforce the risk-reversal message at every evaluation touchpoint. The call-to-action should always confirm that no credit card is needed. Retargeting messaging at this stage should be simpler, not more elaborate — "Still thinking? The first 30 days are completely free."

**What Success Looks Like:**
The merchant completes registration in under two minutes, lands in the product in an expected and welcoming state, and feels immediately ready to begin.

**KPIs:**
- Sign-up form completion rate
- Drop-off point within the sign-up form
- Time to complete registration
- Mobile vs desktop registration ratio

**Opportunities for Automation:**
An abandoned sign-up email: if the merchant starts the form but does not complete it, a gentle reminder within 24 hours. "You were this close to setting up your loyalty programme."

**Possible Future AI Assistance:** [V2 IDEA] Smart form that pre-fills business name and type based on Google Business data for merchants who connect their Google account.

### Stage 4 — Trial

**Merchant Goal:** Land in the product, understand what to do next, and begin setting up without needing help.

**Merchant Thoughts:**
- "Okay, I'm in. What do I do first?"
- "This better not be complicated."
- "I hope I don't have to watch a video just to get started."
- "Let me see if this is as simple as they said."

**Merchant Questions:**
- "Where do I start?"
- "What does this screen mean?"
- "How long will this take?"
- "Is there someone I can ask if I get stuck?"

**Merchant Emotions:** Alert and evaluating. The merchant is mentally running an experiment — they gave OneMember an opportunity and they are watching to see whether it will be worthy of their time. The first 60 seconds of the product experience either confirms or breaks the brand promise made in marketing.

**Merchant Pain Points:**
- An empty product with no guidance on where to begin — the blank-state problem
- Too much choice on the first screen — multiple calls-to-action create paralysis
- A welcome experience that is too long before anything happens — merchants want to do, not watch
- Language that does not match their context — seeing "configure your loyalty programme parameters" instead of "set up your rewards"

**Merchant Risks:**
- Abandons within the first five minutes because the product does not match the simplicity promised
- Completes some steps but does not finish the wizard — leaves with a half-configured account that never gets used
- Finishes the wizard but never adds a real customer — the account sits idle until the trial expires

**What OneMember Should Communicate:**
Direction. Immediately and clearly: "Start here. This is step one of six. It takes 10 minutes." The wizard removes ambiguity by presenting a structured path. The merchant should never have to decide what to do next.

**What the UI Should Achieve:**
The onboarding wizard activates immediately after registration. It guides the merchant through business setup, preferences, loyalty type selection, and starter campaign creation in a linear, distraction-free flow. Progress is visible at all times. The wizard cannot be failed — every decision has a sensible default.

**What Support Should Do:**
Ensure a welcome email arrives within minutes of registration. The email should not overload the merchant with information — it should confirm that they are signed up and tell them exactly what to do next. A link to a short help article for the most common first question ("what type of loyalty programme should I choose?") is appropriate.

**What Marketing Should Do:**
The first email in the onboarding sequence should land within 5 minutes of registration. Subsequent emails follow a schedule based on product events, not fixed time delays. If the merchant completed the wizard, the email acknowledges it. If they did not, the email nudges them back.

**What Success Looks Like:**
The merchant completes the onboarding wizard in one session and ends that session with an active campaign ready for their first real customer.

**KPIs:**
- Wizard start rate (% of registrations that begin the wizard)
- Wizard completion rate (% that finish all steps)
- Wizard step at which drop-off most commonly occurs
- Time from registration to wizard completion

**Opportunities for Automation:**
A wizard-abandoned email sequence: if a merchant starts the wizard but does not complete it within 24 hours, a triggered email asks "Did you get stuck?" with a direct link back to where they left off.

**Possible Future AI Assistance:** [V2 IDEA] A setup assistant that recommends campaign type (points vs stamps) based on the merchant's stated business type and industry, reducing the one decision in the wizard that creates the most hesitation.

### Stage 5 — Onboarding

**Merchant Goal:** Configure the business profile, select a campaign type, and emerge with a working loyalty programme.

**Merchant Thoughts:**
- "What type of loyalty programme is right for my business?"
- "I want to get this done before I open tomorrow."
- "My staff need to be able to use this. I hope it's simple for them too."
- "What happens if I set something up wrong? Can I change it later?"

**Merchant Questions:**
- "Should I use points or stamps?"
- "How many stamps should I require before a reward?"
- "What reward should I offer?"
- "Can I change this later?"

**Merchant Emotions:** Purposeful and slightly uncertain. The merchant is making real decisions — decisions that will determine what customers experience. The uncertainty is positive: it means the merchant is engaged. The risk is that uncertainty becomes anxiety and they abandon.

**Merchant Pain Points:**
- The points-vs-stamps decision is the single most common point of hesitation in the wizard. Merchants know what they want to achieve but do not always know which model fits their business
- Setting a reward that feels "right" — too generous feels expensive, too stingy feels worthless
- Concern that any early configuration decision is permanent

**Merchant Risks:**
- Gets stuck on the loyalty type decision and closes the browser
- Chooses a reward that is so conservative it generates no meaningful engagement
- Completes the wizard but does not feel confident enough to show it to their first customer

**What OneMember Should Communicate:**
Reassurance and guidance. Explain the difference between points and stamps in one sentence each. Give examples from their specific industry. Make the recommendation clear. Confirm that everything can be edited later. Celebrate when the wizard is complete — "Your loyalty programme is live."

**What the UI Should Achieve:**
Each wizard step has one decision to make. Context is provided inline — a short explanation of why this decision matters, with a recommendation for most business types. The final wizard step is a celebration: the merchant sees their campaign live, their member count at zero, and a clear next action: "Add your first member."

**What Support Should Do:**
Proactively prepare short help content for the two most common onboarding questions: "Points or stamps?" and "What reward should I offer?" These should be accessible from within the wizard without requiring the merchant to leave the flow.

**What Marketing Should Do:**
Send a "wizard complete" email that celebrates the milestone and provides the single next action: "Your programme is ready. Add your first member." Keep it brief. The merchant has just spent 10 minutes completing a task — they do not want to read a long email.

**What Success Looks Like:**
The merchant exits the wizard with an active campaign, a clear understanding of how the loyalty programme works, and the confidence to add their first real customer the next time they open OneMember.

**KPIs:**
- Campaign type selected (points vs stamps ratio, per industry)
- Wizard completion time
- Starter campaign active rate (% of completed wizards with an active campaign)
- Rate of post-wizard campaign edits within 24 hours (indicates configuration uncertainty)

**Opportunities for Automation:**
If the merchant edits their campaign within one hour of completing the wizard, trigger a help email: "Made a change? Here's how to think about your reward settings."

**Possible Future AI Assistance:** [V2 IDEA] A recommendation engine that suggests optimal campaign type, stamp count or points-per-spend ratio, and reward value based on the merchant's industry and stated business size.

### Stage 6 — Activation

**Merchant Goal:** Add the first real member and record the first real purchase. Make the loyalty programme operational.

**Merchant Thoughts:**
- "Time to try this with a real customer."
- "I hope it's fast enough — I have customers waiting."
- "I wonder what my customer will think when I ask for their name."
- "If this goes smoothly, I'll do it with every customer today."

**Merchant Questions:**
- "What do I say to my customer when I add them?"
- "What if my customer doesn't want to give their details?"
- "How do I look up a member next time they come in?"
- "How do I record a purchase if the amount changes each visit?"

**Merchant Emotions:** Excited and slightly nervous. This is the first real use of the product with a real person. The merchant is performing something new in front of a customer. If it goes smoothly, confidence accelerates. If it is awkward, they may hesitate to use it again.

**Merchant Pain Points:**
- The counter workflow — adding a member takes 30 seconds but if it feels awkward in front of a customer, the merchant will hesitate to do it
- Not knowing what to say to customers when introducing the loyalty programme
- Any loading delay at the counter feels longer than it is — speed is critical

**Merchant Risks:**
- The merchant adds themselves or a test member but never adds a real customer — the account remains inactive
- The first attempt at the counter goes awkwardly and the merchant loses confidence
- The first real customer declines to join — merchant interprets this as a product failure rather than a normal rate of opt-out

**What OneMember Should Communicate:**
Immediate next action. After the wizard, the dashboard should show a clear empty state: "You're ready. Add your first member." Not a generic empty state — a directed one. Optionally include suggested language the merchant can use with their customers: "We have a new rewards programme. Can I take your name and phone number?"

**What the UI Should Achieve:**
The member add flow must complete in under 30 seconds. Name and phone number are sufficient to start. The points or stamps appear on screen immediately after recording a purchase — the merchant can see the mechanism working in real time.

**What Support Should Do:**
Send a short activation guide within 24 hours of wizard completion: "Your next step is to add your first member. Here's exactly how it works at the counter." No more than 150 words. Link to one help video.

**What Marketing Should Do:**
If the merchant has not added a member within 48 hours of completing the wizard, trigger a gentle nudge: "Your loyalty programme is ready. Your next step takes 30 seconds." Provide the direct link to add a member.

**What Success Looks Like:**
The merchant adds at least one real customer and records at least one real purchase within 48 hours of completing the wizard. The activation milestone is the most important short-term predictor of trial retention.

**KPIs:**
- Time from wizard completion to first real member added
- Time from first member to first purchase recorded
- % of merchants who add a member within 24 hours of wizard completion
- % of merchants who add a member within 48 hours of wizard completion

**Opportunities for Automation:**
A triggered email at 24 hours and 48 hours post-wizard if no member has been added. After first member is added, trigger a congratulatory in-app message: "First member added. Well done — you're on your way."

**Possible Future AI Assistance:** [V2 IDEA] A suggested counter script personalised by business type that the merchant can use to introduce the loyalty programme to customers in their own language.

### Stage 7 — Value Realisation

**Merchant Goal:** Witness a tangible outcome from the loyalty programme — a customer returning because of a reward, or a member reaching a milestone.

**Merchant Thoughts:**
- "Someone just came back because of their reward. This is actually working."
- "I can see who my regular customers are for the first time."
- "A member asked about their points today. They're paying attention."
- "I didn't know I had this many regulars until I started tracking it."

**Merchant Questions:**
- "When will my first customer be close to a reward?"
- "How do I know if the programme is working?"
- "Can I see which customers have not come back in a while?"

**Merchant Emotions:** This is the turning point. The emotional shift from "I hope this works" to "this works" is the most important moment in the entire merchant journey. It is the moment when the product stops being an experiment and becomes indispensable. If this moment arrives before the trial ends, conversion to a paid plan is almost certain.

**Merchant Pain Points:**
- If the merchant has few members or low visit frequency, the value realisation moment may not arrive within the 30-day trial — they do not see a redemption because no member has accumulated enough points yet
- The merchant does not know where to look to see evidence of the programme working — the dashboard surfacing this proactively matters

**Merchant Risks:**
- Trial expires before the merchant sees a meaningful loyalty event — the value was real but invisible
- The merchant sees activity on the dashboard but does not connect it to a business outcome
- A first redemption goes unnoticed because the merchant was busy at the time

**What OneMember Should Communicate:**
Make the programme's progress visible at every login. "You have 12 members. 3 are more than halfway to their reward." "A member came back twice this week." The merchant should not have to search for evidence that the programme is working — it should be on the dashboard.

**What the UI Should Achieve:**
The dashboard is the primary value realisation surface. It should show not just today's data but signals of programme health: members approaching a reward, recent activity, top members. Every metric should feel meaningful to a merchant who is not a data analyst.

**What Support Should Do:**
At day 7 of the trial, send a proactive check-in: "How is your programme going?" Keep it brief. The purpose is to surface any friction before it causes a drop-off, and to create a moment of human connection that reinforces the relationship.

**What Marketing Should Do:**
At day 14, send a progress email: "Your loyalty programme is building momentum. Here's what's happened in your first two weeks." Personalise with actual data: members added, purchases recorded. If the merchant has not yet seen a redemption, explain when they might expect one based on typical usage patterns.

**What Success Looks Like:**
The merchant witnesses at least one meaningful loyalty event — a redemption, a member's third visit, a customer mentioning their points — and explicitly associates it with OneMember. This is the Aha Moment described in Section 5.

**KPIs:**
- Time from first member to first redemption
- % of merchants who achieve a first redemption within 30 days
- % of merchants who log in more than 5 times in the first 14 days (engagement signal)
- Dashboard click-through rates on member and campaign data

**Opportunities for Automation:**
A triggered "milestone" notification when a merchant's first member reaches 50% of the stamps or points required for a reward: "One of your members is getting close to their first reward." Creates anticipation of the first redemption.

**Possible Future AI Assistance:** [V2 IDEA] A weekly programme health summary that interprets the merchant's loyalty data in plain language: "Your top member has visited 4 times this month. 2 members haven't been back in 3 weeks."

### Stage 8 — Subscription

**Merchant Goal:** Make the decision to convert from free trial to a paid subscription.

**Merchant Thoughts:**
- "My trial is ending. Do I keep this or let it go?"
- "I have members in here now. I don't want to lose them."
- "Is the cost worth it? Let me think about how much I'd lose without it."
- "I'll upgrade — it's been working and I don't want to start over."

**Merchant Questions:**
- "What happens to my members if I don't upgrade?"
- "What's the difference between the plans?"
- "Can I start on a lower plan and upgrade later?"

**Merchant Emotions:** Practical and slightly apprehensive. The merchant is not primarily emotionally excited at this point — they are doing a rational calculation. But the emotional weight of losing member data, losing progress, losing something they have built is a powerful conversion driver. This is the correct emotional lever — not urgency or fear, but the very real cost of walking away from something that has started to work.

**Merchant Pain Points:**
- Uncertainty about what happens to data if they do not upgrade
- Difficulty comparing plans if the difference is not immediately clear
- Hesitation about the ongoing monthly cost if value has not been fully established yet

**Merchant Risks:**
- Trial expires without the merchant receiving a reminder — they intended to upgrade and forgot
- The merchant moves to the Free plan rather than upgrading and then gradually disengages
- The merchant upgrades to the wrong plan and experiences limit frustration

**What OneMember Should Communicate:**
Data safety first: "Your member data is always safe, regardless of your plan." Then the value of the paid plan relative to what they have built. Then the specific features they have been using during the trial. Make the upgrade feel like protecting something, not buying something new.

**What the UI Should Achieve:**
The trial expiry experience should be calm, not alarming. Show the merchant what they have built: their member count, their campaign activity, their top members. Then show the upgrade path as the natural continuation. The Free plan should be visible as an option — hiding it creates distrust. But the Professional or Starter plan should be visually and contextually the obvious choice.

**What Support Should Do:**
Proactively reach out at day 25 to any merchant who has meaningful activity (members added, purchases recorded) but has not yet upgraded. A brief, personal email — not a template blast — acknowledging what they have built and making the upgrade path clear.

**What Marketing Should Do:**
A trial expiry sequence of three emails: at day 23 (heads up — 7 days remain), day 28 (2 days remain — here's what you've built), day 30 (today is the last day of your Professional trial). Each email is brief, data-personalised where possible, and ends with a single clear CTA.

**What Success Looks Like:**
The merchant upgrades to a paid plan on or before the last day of the trial. The conversion feels natural — not pressured — because the value was established before the trial ended.

**KPIs:**
- Trial-to-paid conversion rate overall
- Trial-to-paid conversion rate segmented by: wizard complete vs incomplete, members added vs not, first redemption achieved vs not
- Day of conversion within the trial (early conversion is a health signal)
- Plan selected at first conversion

**Opportunities for Automation:**

## 4. The First 15 Minutes

The first 15 minutes determine whether the merchant becomes a loyal user or a statistic in an abandonment report. This sequence represents the ideal path. Every design, copy, and technical decision in the product should be evaluated against whether it protects or undermines this sequence.

---

**Minute 0 — Registration Complete**

The merchant has entered their name, email, and password. They click "Create Account."

*What happens:* The account is created instantly. The merchant is redirected to the onboarding wizard. A welcome email is sent in the background.

*Why it matters psychologically:* The merchant has made a micro-commitment. They registered. The product must honour that commitment immediately with a clear, welcoming first screen. Any delay, any confusion, any mismatch from the expectation set in marketing breaks the psychological contract formed at this moment.

*The risk:* A slow load, an error message, or an unclear first screen can cause immediate abandonment. The merchant is in an evaluative mindset — they are asking "was signing up the right decision?"

---

**Minute 1 — Welcome Screen**

The onboarding wizard opens on a welcome screen. The merchant sees their business name (pre-filled from registration), a brief statement of what they are about to do ("Set up your loyalty programme in a few steps"), and a single button: "Let's start."

*What happens:* The wizard begins. Progress is shown — "Step 1 of 6."

*Why it matters psychologically:* The merchant needs to know the task has a defined end. "A few steps" means this is not open-ended. Seeing "Step 1 of 6" means there are exactly six steps and no more. The brain releases anxiety when uncertainty has a boundary.

---

**Minute 2 — Business Info**

The merchant confirms or fills in their business name, business type, and business phone. These fields are short. Most merchants complete this step in under 60 seconds.

*What happens:* The system captures the business type — this will inform the campaign type recommendation in a later step.

*Why it matters psychologically:* The merchant is describing themselves to the system. This creates a sense of personalisation — the product is learning about them, not giving them a generic experience. Simple questions answered quickly build momentum.

---

**Minute 4 — Settings**

The merchant selects their currency, timezone, and date format. Sensible defaults are pre-selected (Thai Baht, Bangkok, DD/MM/YYYY for Thailand). Most merchants accept the defaults and move on.

*What happens:* The settings are saved. The merchant moves to the loyalty type selection step.

*Why it matters psychologically:* Most merchants will not change the defaults. Pre-selecting the right defaults is a design decision that signals "this product was built for someone like me." A merchant who sees "USD" pre-selected in a Thai context immediately feels the product is not designed for them.

---

**Minute 5 — Loyalty Type Selection**

The merchant chooses between Points and Stamps. The wizard provides a one-sentence explanation of each: "Points — customers earn a point for every amount they spend. Stamps — customers collect a stamp for every visit." An inline recommendation appears based on their business type.

*What happens:* The merchant selects their preferred loyalty type. This determines the campaign template used in the next step.

*Why it matters psychologically:* This is the one genuinely uncertain decision in the wizard. A merchant who has never run a loyalty programme does not intuitively know which model fits their business. The recommendation — "Most cafés use Stamps" — resolves the uncertainty without requiring research. The merchant feels guided, not left alone.

---

**Minute 7 — Quick Start (Campaign Created)**

The wizard creates a starter campaign with pre-set defaults based on the merchant's chosen loyalty type. The merchant sees their first campaign — named, configured, and active.

*What happens:* A campaign is created automatically. The merchant can see it exists. They are prompted to optionally adjust the reward before completing the wizard.

*Why it matters psychologically:* For the first time, the merchant sees something real. The campaign is not a form to fill — it is a result. The psychological shift from "setting things up" to "something exists" is significant. The merchant now has something to protect, something to show a customer.

---

**Minute 9 — Wizard Complete**

The wizard concludes with a confirmation screen. "Your loyalty programme is live." The merchant sees a summary of what was created and a single next action: "Add your first member."

*What happens:* The merchant lands on the dashboard for the first time. The campaign is visible. The member count is zero. The empty state is directed: "Your first step: add a member."

*Why it matters psychologically:* Completion of the wizard is the first genuine milestone. The merchant should feel a sense of achievement. The celebration screen — however brief — creates a positive emotional anchor. "Your loyalty programme is live" is not just information; it is an emotional event.

---

**Minute 10 — First Member Added (Ideal path)**

The merchant navigates to Members and adds their first member — typically their own name and number as a test.

*What happens:* The member appears on the list. The points balance or stamp count is zero. The member has a profile.

*Why it matters psychologically:* The merchant sees what a customer will look like in the system. The abstract concept of "managing members" becomes concrete. They can see that this is simple — adding a member is a form with three fields that completes in 20 seconds.

---

**Minute 12 — First Purchase Recorded**

The merchant records a test purchase for the member they just added. They see the points or stamps update instantly on the member's profile.

*What happens:* The transaction is logged. The member's balance reflects the purchase. The dashboard updates.

*Why it matters psychologically:* This is the first demonstration of the loyalty mechanism in action. The merchant just watched points appear — or a stamp get added — in real time. The abstract promise ("your customers earn rewards") became a visible, immediate event. This is the beginning of the Aha Moment described in Section 5.

---

**Minute 13 — Dashboard Returns Evidence**

The merchant returns to the dashboard and sees their first data: one member, one purchase recorded, points issued.

*What happens:* The dashboard KPIs update. The recent activity feed shows the test transaction.

*Why it matters psychologically:* For the first time, the dashboard has real data. The merchant is no longer looking at a demo — they are looking at their own business. Even one member and one transaction makes the product feel operational.

---

**Minute 15 — The Merchant Imagines**

The merchant pauses. They are no longer reading or clicking — they are thinking.

They are thinking about the customer who comes in every Tuesday for coffee. About the client who has been coming to the salon for two years but has never been formally recognised. About the customer who asked last week if the shop had any loyalty rewards.

*What happens:* Nothing happens in the product. But something happens in the merchant's mind. They shift from "does this work?" to "I'm going to use this with my regulars starting tomorrow."

*Why it matters psychologically:* This mental rehearsal — imagining specific customers in a new context — is the sign that the product has landed. The merchant has internalised the value. The experience has moved from evaluation to adoption. No feature, no marketing copy, and no support interaction creates this moment — only a product experience simple enough to allow the imagination to take over.

## 5. The Aha Moment

### Definition

The Aha Moment for an OneMember merchant is the instant they see the loyalty cycle close — when a real customer returns, redeems a reward, or demonstrates that they are aware of and motivated by the programme.

Specifically, it is most commonly one of these three events:

1. **A member redeems their first reward.** A customer walks in and says "I think I have enough for a free coffee." The merchant checks the profile, confirms the redemption, and issues the reward. The abstract promise has become a real transaction.

2. **A member mentions their points or stamps unprompted.** "How many stamps do I have? I'm trying to get to 10." This means the programme is part of the customer's thinking about the business — not just the merchant's.

3. **A returning customer can be looked up by name.** The merchant types a regular customer's name, their full history appears, and for the first time the merchant can see in one place how many times this person has visited and how valuable they are.

### Why These Moments Matter

Each of these events makes a different part of the value proposition real:

- The redemption proves that the loyalty cycle actually closes — that customers earn and return to claim.
- The unprompted mention proves that customers are paying attention — the programme is influencing their behaviour.
- The member lookup proves the member list has value beyond loyalty — it is a record of the merchant's most valuable relationships.

The moment is not the same for every merchant. A café owner may have their Aha Moment on day 4 when a regular mentions their stamp count. A dental clinic may have theirs on day 22 when a patient books their six-month checkup earlier than usual because they want to earn their points before they expire.

### How the Product Should Reinforce It

**Celebrate the first redemption.** When a merchant records their first reward redemption, the product should acknowledge it: a banner, a notification, a moment of recognition. "First redemption recorded. Your loyalty programme is working."

**Surface member engagement signals.** The dashboard should show when members are approaching a reward — "3 members are 1 stamp away from their reward." This creates anticipation of the Aha Moment before it arrives.

**Log the loyalty lifecycle visibly.** The Recent Activity feed shows every transaction. Seeing "Sarah — Stamps: 8/10" on the dashboard gives the merchant a sense of a story unfolding — a customer who is about to reach a milestone.

**Do not rush past the moment.** When a redemption is processed, the product should pause — not literally, but experientially. A confirmation screen that says "Reward redeemed. [Customer name] has earned their reward." is more memorable than a silent state change.

## 6. Critical Success Moments

These are the discrete events that most strongly predict whether a merchant will stay with OneMember long-term. Missing any of them is a measurable risk.

---

**First Campaign Created**

*Why it matters:* A merchant who creates a campaign has made an investment — they have designed a loyalty experience. A merchant who does not create a campaign never has anything to show customers.

*Risk if missed:* The account sits idle. The merchant has no loyalty programme to operate and no reason to return to the product.

*Recommended communication:* If the merchant completes registration but does not complete the wizard within 24 hours, send a direct nudge: "Your loyalty programme isn't live yet. It takes 10 minutes. Here's where you left off."

---

**First Member Added**

*Why it matters:* The first member is proof that the merchant has connected the product to their real business. Without members, nothing else can happen.

*Risk if missed:* The account has a campaign but no members — the loyalty cycle cannot begin. A merchant in this state is very likely to let the trial expire.

*Recommended communication:* A triggered email at 24 hours post-wizard if no member exists: "Your programme is ready. Your next step takes 30 seconds." Include a direct link to the Add Member page.

---

**First Purchase Recorded**

*Why it matters:* The first purchase proves the counter workflow works. The merchant has stood at their business, taken out a phone or opened a laptop, looked up a customer, and recorded a transaction. Every subsequent purchase is easier because the first one succeeded.

*Risk if missed:* The member exists but has no transactions. The loyalty mechanism never activates.

*Recommended communication:* A triggered email at 48 hours post-first-member if no purchase exists: "You've added a member — great start. The next step is to give them their first points. Here's how."

---

**First Reward Configured**

*Why it matters:* A campaign without a reward configured is an incomplete loyalty programme. The reward is the destination the customer is working toward.

*Risk if missed:* Members accumulate points or stamps but there is nothing to redeem them for. This creates a perception that the programme is broken.

*Recommended communication:* Built into the wizard — reward configuration is part of the Quick Start step. If a merchant edits or deletes their starter reward without replacing it, a notification should prompt them to configure a replacement.

---

**First Redemption**

*Why it matters:* This is the Aha Moment described in Section 5. It is the single event most strongly correlated with long-term merchant retention.

*Risk if missed:* If the merchant does not see a redemption during the 30-day trial, they may conclude the programme is not working — even if members are accumulating stamps or points at a normal pace.

*Recommended communication:* In-product celebration when the first redemption is recorded. An email within hours: "Congratulations — a customer just claimed their first reward."

---

**First Returning Customer**

*Why it matters:* A returning member — someone who visited, earned, and came back — is the first real evidence that the loyalty programme is influencing customer behaviour.

*Risk if missed:* If members join but do not return, the merchant's programme is functioning as a registration tool, not a retention tool.

*Recommended communication:* An in-product notification when a member visits for a second time: "A returning member just earned more [stamps/points]." Creates a visible event out of something the merchant might otherwise not notice.

---

**Trial Day 7**

*Why it matters:* At day 7, the merchant has had one full week with the product. Merchants who are active at day 7 are significantly more likely to convert than those who are not.

*Risk if missed:* A merchant who has not logged in after day 3 is at high risk of trial expiry without conversion.

*Recommended communication:* A day-7 check-in email. Brief and personal. If they have been active, celebrate their progress. If they have not, acknowledge the gap and offer a simple path back.

---

**Trial Day 21**

*Why it matters:* Nine days before trial expiry. A merchant who is active and has meaningful data at day 21 should receive their subscription decision prompt now — not at day 29 when urgency creates pressure.

*Risk if missed:* Leaving the subscription conversation until the last days of the trial creates a pressure dynamic that feels transactional rather than natural.

*Recommended communication:* A day-21 email that summarises what the merchant has built: member count, purchases recorded, top member. Then presents the paid plan options as the natural continuation.

---

**Trial Expiry**

*Why it matters:* The trial's last day is the final conversion opportunity. A merchant who was active but did not upgrade needs one more prompt.

*Risk if missed:* The merchant intended to upgrade, forgot, and moves to the Free plan by default. Some will re-engage; many will gradually disengage as the product becomes less useful on the Free tier.

*Recommended communication:* A same-day email on trial expiry day. Clear subject: "Your 30-day trial ends today." Brief body summarising what they have built, what happens next, and a single upgrade CTA.

---

**First Paid Month**

*Why it matters:* A merchant who pays once is significantly more likely to pay again. The first payment is a renewed commitment — the merchant has chosen OneMember over doing nothing.

*Risk if missed:* A failed payment on the first billing cycle can cause immediate churn if the recovery path is not clear.

*Recommended communication:* A payment confirmation email on the first billing date. Brief and warm: "Your subscription is active. Your loyalty programme keeps running." No urgency, no noise — just confirmation.

---

**Second Location**

*Why it matters:* A merchant opening a second location is a major lifecycle event and a strong upsell signal. They need OneMember to work across two sites.

*Risk if missed:* The merchant tries to adapt the current product to a multi-location use case that it does not yet support, fails, and loses confidence.

*Recommended communication:* A proactive support article or email addressing the second-location use case — what is currently possible, what the roadmap holds, how the merchant can get the most out of the current product while multi-location is developed.

---

**Staff Invitation**

*Why it matters:* When a merchant invites a staff member to use OneMember, the product becomes embedded in the business — not just the owner's personal tool. Staff-adopted products churn at much lower rates.

*Risk if missed:* If only the owner uses the product and the owner is away, the loyalty programme pauses. Staff access creates operational resilience.

*Recommended communication:* At the 30-day mark, if the merchant is a paid subscriber with active usage, prompt them to consider adding a staff account (when available): "Your team can help run your loyalty programme. Here's how to invite them."

## 7. Drop-off Risks

Drop-off occurs when a merchant stops engaging with the product before achieving the next critical success moment. Understanding where and why drop-off happens allows the product, marketing, and support to intervene.

---

**Drop-off 1: Between Registration and Wizard Completion**

*Where it happens:* Merchant signs up but does not start or complete the wizard.

*Possible reasons:*
- Registered at a busy moment — intended to return later and forgot
- First screen was not clear enough to know what to do next
- The wizard started and felt longer than expected
- A staff member called or a customer arrived mid-flow

*How the product can reduce it:*
The wizard auto-saves progress at every step. Returning to the product after an interrupted session should resume exactly where the merchant left off, not start again.

*How marketing can reduce it:*
A triggered email at 2 hours and 24 hours if the wizard is not completed. Subject lines that do not create pressure: "Your loyalty programme is ready to be set up. It takes 10 minutes."

*How support can reduce it:*
Make the "did you get stuck?" email available at 24 hours for merchants who started but did not complete the wizard.

---

**Drop-off 2: Between Wizard Completion and First Real Member**

*Where it happens:* The merchant finishes the wizard but never adds a real customer.

*Possible reasons:*
- Finished the wizard in the evening when the shop was closed — intended to add a member the next morning and forgot
- Felt uncertain about what to say to customers when introducing the programme
- Added a test member (their own details) and considered the task complete
- The wizard was completed for curiosity and the merchant never intended to use the product in earnest

*How the product can reduce it:*
The post-wizard empty state is directed, not generic. "Your loyalty programme is ready. Add your first member." A single, prominent CTA with a direct link. The empty state should not feel like failure — it should feel like step one is complete and step two is waiting.

*How marketing can reduce it:*
Triggered emails at 24 and 48 hours post-wizard. Include a practical tip: "What to say when you introduce the programme to your customers."

*How support can reduce it:*
A short, practical guide to the counter conversation — the exact moment that feels most uncertain for new merchants.

---

**Drop-off 3: Between First Member and First Redemption**

*Where it happens:* The merchant is active — adding members, recording purchases — but the trial expires before any member reaches the reward threshold.

*Possible reasons:*
- Points or stamp requirement set too high relative to visit frequency
- Low trial-period visit frequency for the industry (dental clinics, spas) means the loyalty cycle is inherently longer than 30 days
- The merchant loses momentum after the first week without a visible "this is working" signal

*How the product can reduce it:*
Surface progress signals on the dashboard: "3 members are more than halfway to their reward." Show the merchant that the programme is working even before the first redemption arrives.

*How marketing can reduce it:*
A day-14 email that explicitly addresses the first-redemption expectation: "Your members are building up their stamps. Based on typical visit patterns, you can expect your first redemption in the next few weeks."

*How support can reduce it:*
If the merchant contacts support expressing uncertainty about whether the programme is working, proactively review their account and provide specific data: "You have 12 members. 3 of them have visited twice. Here's what to expect next."

---

**Drop-off 4: Trial Expiry Without Conversion**

*Where it happens:* The merchant reaches day 30 having used the product but without upgrading to a paid plan.

*Possible reasons:*
- The value was not clearly felt — no redemption, few members
- The merchant did not receive a clear upgrade prompt at the right moment
- Price sensitivity — even with value demonstrated, the monthly cost felt unjustified
- The merchant forgot — they intended to upgrade and missed the deadline

*How the product can reduce it:*
A visible in-product trial countdown in the last 7 days. Not alarming — informational. "7 days remaining in your Professional trial."

*How marketing can reduce it:*
A three-email expiry sequence: day 23, day 28, day 30. Each email personalised with the merchant's activity data. The day-30 email is the last opportunity — it must be clear, warm, and direct.

*How support can reduce it:*
A proactive outreach at day 25 for merchants with significant activity (5+ members, 10+ purchases) who have not yet upgraded. The message acknowledges what they have built and makes upgrading feel like protecting it.

---

**Drop-off 5: Post-Conversion Churn (Month 1–3)**

*Where it happens:* The merchant pays for the first month but cancels within 90 days.

*Possible reasons:*
- Paid conversion was driven by sunk-cost logic ("I built something") rather than genuine value realisation
- The first redemption never occurred — the programme never felt like it worked
- Staff adopted the product inconsistently — the merchant was the only user and usage dropped when they were away
- A competing tool was tried and preferred

*How the product can reduce it:*
A monthly usage health score visible to the merchant: "Your programme is healthy — your members are active." If health drops, an in-product prompt: "Your member activity has been quiet recently. Here's how to re-engage your loyal customers."

*How marketing can reduce it:*
Monthly progress emails that celebrate milestones. At 60 days paid, send a "your first two months" summary — even modest activity feels significant when summarised.

*How support can reduce it:*
A 30-day post-conversion check-in from the support team: "You've been a subscriber for a month. How is it going? Is there anything we can help you with?"

## 8. Emotional Journey

The merchant's emotional state changes significantly throughout the journey. Understanding these transitions allows every team to respond appropriately.

---

**Phase 1 — Confused**

*When it occurs:* Before the merchant fully understands what OneMember does or whether it is relevant to them.

*What it feels like for the merchant:* "I've heard about loyalty programmes but I don't know where to start. Is this even something for a business my size?"

*What creates the transition out of this phase:* A clear, specific message that names their business type and describes a familiar problem. The moment the merchant thinks "that sounds exactly like what I'm dealing with," the confusion lifts.

*What can trap the merchant here:* Generic SaaS language. A homepage that does not mention their industry. A product description that talks about features rather than problems.

---

**Phase 2 — Curious**

*When it occurs:* The merchant has understood the concept and is investigating whether OneMember specifically is the right tool.

*What it feels like:* "This might be what I need. Let me see what it actually looks like."

*What creates the transition:* Specific social proof (similar industry, similar size), visible pricing, a no-pressure trial offer, and a clear answer to "do my customers need an app?" Curiosity converts to interest when questions get answered without asking for them.

*What can trap the merchant here:* Unanswered questions before sign-up. Pricing hidden behind a sales call. Demo requirements. Anything that adds friction to the natural momentum of investigation.

---

**Phase 3 — Hopeful**

*When it occurs:* The merchant has signed up and is completing the wizard. They have invested time and made a choice.

*What it feels like:* "I hope this is as simple as they said. I hope this is going to work for my business."

*What creates the transition:* A wizard that delivers exactly what was promised — short, clear, with sensible defaults. The first time the merchant sees their campaign created and active, hope becomes confidence.

*What can trap the merchant here:* A wizard that is longer or more confusing than expected. Unclear language. A decision that requires knowledge the merchant does not have (without guidance). A technical error.

---

**Phase 4 — Excited**

*When it occurs:* The merchant has a working programme and is adding real customers.

*What it feels like:* "I just told my first customer about it and they said 'I'll definitely sign up.' This might actually work."

*What creates the transition:* The first real counter interaction — adding a member in front of a real customer — going smoothly. The customer's positive reaction. Seeing points or stamps appear on screen. The product doing exactly what it promised.

*What can trap the merchant here:* A first customer who reacts negatively or indifferently. A counter workflow that is too slow. A product error in front of a customer.

---

**Phase 5 — Confident**

*When it occurs:* The merchant has used the product consistently for 1–2 weeks. The routine is established. Staff have been shown how it works.

*What it feels like:* "I know how this works. My team knows how this works. We're doing this every day."

*What creates the transition:* Repetition. The merchant has added enough members and recorded enough purchases that the workflow is automatic. The product feels like a part of the business, not an experiment.

*What can trap the merchant here:* An error or inconsistency that breaks the routine. A product update that changes something the merchant relied on. A staff member who resists using the product.

---

**Phase 6 — Successful**

*When it occurs:* The merchant sees a concrete outcome — a redemption, a returning member, dashboard data that confirms the programme is working.

*What it feels like:* "A customer just came back because of their reward. I can see it on the dashboard. This is actually working."

*What creates the transition:* The Aha Moment. A real loyalty event that the merchant can point to and say "that happened because of OneMember."

*What can trap the merchant here:* Nothing, usually. Once a merchant reaches the Successful phase, they are very likely to convert and remain. The risk is primarily that this phase is not reached before trial expiry.

---

**Phase 7 — Proud**

*When it occurs:* The merchant has been using the product for several weeks. They have a member base. Their top customers are visible on the dashboard. The programme feels like a professional achievement.

*What it feels like:* "I'm proud of this. I'm running a proper loyalty programme. My customers notice."

*What creates the transition:* Accumulated success. The member list growing. Redemptions becoming routine. The merchant beginning to identify themselves as a business that runs a loyalty programme — not just a business that tried a loyalty app.

*What can trap the merchant here:* Very little at this stage. Churn from the Proud phase is typically caused by external factors (business closure, competitor programme, budget change) rather than product dissatisfaction.

---

**Phase 8 — Advocate**

*When it occurs:* The merchant has told someone else about OneMember — unprompted, because they wanted to, because the product made a visible difference.

*What it feels like:* "I've been recommending this to everyone. My friend who runs the nail salon is going to try it next week."

*What creates the transition:* The desire to share a solution that worked. Advocacy is not manufactured — it is the natural result of a merchant reaching the Proud phase with an easy way to share. The referral mechanism and the quality of the product experience determine how many Proud merchants become Advocates.

*What can trap the merchant here:* Nothing stops advocacy — but friction in the referral path (no easy link to share, no incentive, no awareness that referrals are welcomed) means that Proud merchants who would have referred do not, because acting on the intention was too much work.

## 9. Communication Plan

The following matrix defines the right communication format, message, and timing at each stage of the journey.

---

### Stage-by-Stage Communication

**Awareness**

| Format | Message | What to Avoid |
|--------|---------|---------------|
| Social media ads | Problem-first. "Customers left and never came back? OneMember fixes that." | Listing features. Mentioning "software." Technical language. |
| Google search ads | Intent-matching. "Loyalty programme for cafés in Thailand. Set up in 10 minutes." | Generic claims. "Best loyalty platform." Unsupported superlatives. |
| SEO content | Educational. "How to keep customers coming back to your café." | Product-first framing. Sales language in editorial content. |

---

**Interest**

| Format | Message | What to Avoid |
|--------|---------|---------------|
| Website homepage | Outcome headline. "Turn Every Customer Into a Regular." Industry list visible. | Jargon. Feature lists without outcomes. Pricing hidden. |
| Industry landing pages | "For [Salons / Cafés / Gyms]: here's exactly how OneMember works." | "Our solution" language. Technical architecture descriptions. |
| Pricing page | Transparent plan comparison. Free trial clearly highlighted. | Fine print. Confusing plan names. Limiting the Free tier description so merchants distrust it. |

---

**Evaluation**

| Format | Message | What to Avoid |
|--------|---------|---------------|
| Sign-up page | "Start free. No credit card. No commitment." Three fields maximum. | Long forms. Password complexity requirements before the account is created. |
| FAQ | Short answers to the 10 most common pre-signup questions. | Walls of text. Answers that create new questions. |
| Registration confirmation email | "You're in. Here's your next step." One step, not ten. | A long welcome email that arrives before the merchant has done anything. |

---

**Trial (First 24 hours)**

| Format | Message | What to Avoid |
|--------|---------|---------------|
| Welcome email (sent within 5 minutes) | "Your account is ready. Start your setup — it takes 10 minutes." | A generic "welcome" email with no specific next action. |
| Onboarding wizard in-product | "Step 1 of 6. You're getting started." Progress visible at all times. | Wizard without a progress indicator. A wizard that can be "failed." |
| Tooltip (wizard) | Contextual help on the loyalty type selection step: "Most cafés use Stamps. Most restaurants use Points." | Tooltips on obvious fields. Tooltips that appear uninvited on every click. |

---

**Onboarding (Day 1–2)**

| Format | Message | What to Avoid |
|--------|---------|---------------|
| Wizard completion email | "Your loyalty programme is live. Your next step: add your first member." | Multiple next actions. A long email full of features. |
| Activation nudge (if no member at 24 hours) | "Your programme is ready. Your first member takes 30 seconds to add." | Pressure language. Urgency that feels pushy. |
| Help article | "How to introduce your loyalty programme to customers." In plain language. | A help article written for a technical audience. |

---

**Activation (Day 2–7)**

| Format | Message | What to Avoid |
|--------|---------|---------------|
| First member added (in-product) | "First member added. Your loyalty programme has started." | Silence. Missing the milestone entirely. |
| First purchase recorded (in-product) | "First purchase recorded. [Member name] has earned [X] points/stamps." | A generic transaction confirmation. Showing the technical event, not the human outcome. |
| Day 7 email | "How is your first week going? Here's what your programme looks like." | A promotional email about upgrading at day 7. Too early. |

---

**Value Realisation (Day 7–21)**

| Format | Message | What to Avoid |
|--------|---------|---------------|
| First redemption (in-product) | "First reward redeemed. Your loyalty programme is working." | No celebration. A silent state change. |
| Day 14 email | "Your programme is building momentum. Here's a summary of your first two weeks." | Data-heavy report. Technical metrics without meaning. |
| Dashboard | "3 members are approaching their reward. Expect your first redemption soon." | A dashboard that shows only current state with no forward-looking signals. |

---

**Subscription (Day 21–30)**

| Format | Message | What to Avoid |
|--------|---------|---------------|
| Day 21 email | "9 days left in your trial. Here's what you've built. Here's how to keep it." | Pressure. Countdown language. |
| Day 25 personal outreach (for active merchants) | "You've built something real here. Let's talk about keeping it going." | A template blast. Identical messaging for all merchants regardless of activity. |
| Day 28 email | "2 days left. Your [12] members are counting on you." | Fear-based language. "Your data will be deleted." |
| Day 30 email | "Today is your last day. Upgrade now to keep everything exactly as it is." | Aggressive urgency. Hidden downgrade consequences. |

---

**Expansion (Month 2 onwards)**

| Format | Message | What to Avoid |
|--------|---------|---------------|
| Monthly progress email | "Your loyalty programme this month: [12 new members], [47 purchases], [3 redemptions]." | Generic newsletter. Promotional content. |
| Milestone in-product messages | "50 members! You've built something impressive. Here's what the best programmes do next." | Milestone ignored. No acknowledgement of growth. |
| Help articles | Advanced guides: "How to grow your member base," "How to train staff on OneMember." | Advanced content surfaced too early (before the merchant is ready). |

---

**Advocacy**

| Format | Message | What to Avoid |
|--------|---------|---------------|
| Referral prompt (in-product) | "Know another business that should be doing this? Share OneMember." | A pop-up that interrupts the merchant mid-task. |
| NPS survey (Month 3) | "How likely are you to recommend OneMember to another business owner?" | A long survey. NPS buried in a feature request form. |
| Referral success email | "Your referral just signed up. Thank you — here's your reward." | Delayed acknowledgement. An uncommunicated referral benefit. |

## 10. Success Metrics

These metrics should be measured from day one of commercial launch. Targets should not be set until there is real merchant data to establish baselines. Every metric listed here is measurable within the product's existing data model.

---

### Acquisition Metrics

| Metric | Definition | Why It Matters |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| **Registration rate** | % of website visitors who complete sign-up | Measures how effectively the website converts interest to commitment |
| **Sign-up form completion rate** | % of merchants who start the form and complete it | Identifies friction in the registration flow |
| **Time to registration** | Seconds from landing on sign-up page to account created | A proxy for sign-up friction |

---

### Onboarding Metrics

| Metric | Definition | Why It Matters |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| **Wizard start rate** | % of registrations that begin the wizard | Measures the effectiveness of the post-registration experience |
| **Wizard completion rate** | % of wizard starts that complete all steps | The single most important early signal of product-market fit |
| **Time to wizard completion** | Minutes from wizard start to completion | Identifies steps that create disproportionate time investment |
| **Wizard drop-off step** | The specific wizard step most commonly abandoned | Guides targeted UX improvement |

---

### Activation Metrics

| Metric | Definition | Why It Matters |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| **Time to first member** | Hours from wizard completion to first real member added | The key leading indicator of trial retention |
| **Time to first purchase** | Hours from first member to first purchase recorded | Measures counter workflow adoption |
| **Time to first redemption** | Days from sign-up to first reward redeemed | Measures how quickly the merchant experiences the Aha Moment |
| **Activation rate** | % of trial merchants who add at least one member and one purchase | The composite activation metric |

---

### Retention Metrics

| Metric | Definition | Why It Matters |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| **Trial-to-paid conversion rate** | % of trial merchants who subscribe before or at day 30 | The most important commercial metric |
| **Day 7 active rate** | % of registrations who log in at least once during days 5–7 | Early engagement predictor of conversion |
| **Day 14 active rate** | % of registrations who log in at least once during days 12–14 | Sustained engagement predictor |
| **30-day retention rate** | % of paying merchants still active at day 30 post-conversion | First retention measurement |
| **90-day retention rate** | % of paying merchants still active at day 90 post-conversion | The leading indicator of long-term health |

---

### Growth Metrics

| Metric | Definition | Why It Matters |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| **Members added per merchant per month** | Average across active merchants | Measures how actively merchants use the loyalty programme |
| **Purchases recorded per merchant per month** | Average across active merchants | Measures daily operational engagement with the product |
| **Redemptions per merchant per month** | Average across active merchants | The closest proxy to merchant-perceived value |
| **Campaign creation rate** | % of merchants with 2 or more active campaigns | Measures depth of product adoption |

---

### Advocacy Metrics

| Metric | Definition | Why It Matters |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| **Merchant NPS** | Net Promoter Score collected at months 1, 3, and 6 | The single best proxy for advocacy and long-term retention |
| **Referral rate** | % of new sign-ups attributed to an existing merchant | The highest-trust acquisition signal |
| **Referral conversion rate** | % of referred visitors who complete sign-up | Measures the quality of referral intent |

## 11. Future Automation Opportunities

All items in this section are post-launch ideas. They are not V1.0 requirements. They are logged here to guide future roadmap decisions.

---

**[V2 IDEA] Intelligent Onboarding Assistant**
A conversational prompt during the wizard that adapts guidance based on the merchant's business type. "You're setting up a café. Most cafés use Stamps — one per visit, with the 10th free. Would you like to start with that?" Reduces wizard hesitation on the loyalty type selection step.

**[V2 IDEA] Merchant Health Score**
An automated metric calculated from: login frequency, members added per month, purchases recorded per week, redemptions issued, and days since last login. Used internally to identify at-risk merchants before they churn, and surfaced to the merchant as a simple "your programme is healthy / needs attention" indicator.

**[V2 IDEA] Automated Milestone Celebrations**
Triggered in-product messages and emails at merchant milestones: first redemption, 10 members, 50 members, 100 members, 1 year as a subscriber. Each milestone generates a brief, personal acknowledgement and a practical next step. Milestone emails have high open rates and reinforce the merchant's emotional connection to the product.

**[V2 IDEA] Campaign Performance Recommendations**
After the merchant has been running a programme for 60 days, an automated analysis of whether the stamp count or points threshold appears optimal. "Most of your members are reaching their reward in 8–10 visits. Your current setting of 15 stamps may be discouraging some customers from completing their card. Here's what we'd suggest." Positions OneMember as an active partner in the merchant's success.

**[V2 IDEA] Birthday Campaign Suggestions**
A proactive prompt at day 30 for merchants who have not yet configured a birthday reward: "You have 24 members with birthdays recorded. A birthday reward takes 2 minutes to set up and is the highest-impact single feature for member loyalty." Drives adoption of a feature that is strongly correlated with merchant satisfaction.

**[V2 IDEA] Lapsed Member Alerts**
A weekly digest that tells the merchant which members have not returned within their expected frequency: "5 of your regular members haven't been in for over 4 weeks. Here's who they are." Gives the merchant actionable intelligence without requiring them to analyse their own data.

**[V2 IDEA] Win-back Campaign Builder**
When a merchant's member base shows a significant lapse rate, an automated prompt to create a win-back campaign: "15 of your members haven't visited in 2 months. Consider a double-stamps day or a bonus points event to bring them back." Tied to V2 campaign automation features.

**[V2 IDEA] Churn Prediction and Intervention**
An AI model trained on merchant behaviour patterns that identifies merchants likely to cancel 30 days before they do. Used to trigger a proactive support outreach or a feature recommendation that addresses the likely pain point driving the cancellation intent.

**[V2 IDEA] Optimal Timing Recommendations**
For merchants who send birthday rewards manually, an AI assistant that suggests the optimal day to award the reward based on the member's typical visit pattern. "Sarah's birthday is on the 15th. She typically visits on Fridays. Her birthday falls on a Thursday this year — consider triggering her reward a day early so she can use it on her usual visit day."

**[V2 IDEA] Referral Network Suggestions**
Based on the merchant's industry and location, an AI recommendation of which types of businesses in their network would benefit from OneMember. "You run a hair salon. Based on our merchant network, nearby nail salons, gyms, and cafés are the most likely to benefit from OneMember. Do you know any?" Makes referral intentions concrete and actionable.

## 13. Continuous Improvement Framework

This blueprint is a living document. The merchant experience changes as the product evolves, as new merchants join, and as usage patterns become clearer. The following framework defines how this blueprint should be maintained.

### Sources of Improvement Intelligence

**Merchant Interviews**
Conduct structured interviews with 5–10 merchants per quarter. Focus on two questions: "When did you first feel that OneMember was working for you?" and "Was there a moment when you almost stopped using it?" These interviews are the most reliable source of qualitative journey insight.

**Support Ticket Analysis**
Review incoming support tickets monthly. Categorise by journey stage and type of problem. A cluster of tickets about the same step in the wizard is a product signal. A cluster of tickets about the same feature is a documentation or UX signal. Support tickets are the closest proxy to where the merchant experience is failing.

**Analytics Review**
Monitor the key metrics defined in Section 10 weekly for the first three months, then monthly. Pay particular attention to wizard drop-off rates, time-to-first-member, and trial conversion rate. These metrics tell you whether the theoretical journey described in this blueprint is actually being experienced.

**Feature Usage Analysis**
Track which product features are used, in what sequence, and at what stage of the journey. Features used by more than 70% of active merchants are core to the experience. Features used by fewer than 20% should be investigated: are they undiscoverable, unnecessary, or simply premature in the journey?

**Customer Feedback Collection**
An NPS survey delivered in-product at months 1, 3, and 6 post-conversion. Keep it to one question plus one optional open text field. Analyse the open text responses alongside the score — they are often more actionable than the number.

**Churn Analysis**
When a merchant cancels, attempt to understand why. A brief cancellation survey (one question: "What was the main reason you cancelled?") with 3–4 options captures the most common signals. Analyse cancellation reasons monthly and group them by journey stage and merchant type.

---

### Review Cadence

| Review Type | Frequency | Owner | Participants |
|-------------|-----------|-------|-------------|
| Metrics review | Weekly (first 3 months), monthly thereafter | Product Owner | Product, Marketing |
| Support ticket analysis | Monthly | Customer Success | Product, Support |
| Blueprint update | Quarterly | Product Owner | Product, Marketing, Customer Success |
| Full merchant interview cycle | Quarterly | Customer Success | Product Owner, Support |
| Annual blueprint revision | Annual (or after major product release) | Product Owner | All teams |

---

### When to Update This Blueprint

Update a section of this blueprint when:

1. A metrics review reveals that a journey stage is performing significantly differently from what the blueprint predicts
2. A pattern of support tickets indicates friction at a stage not currently identified as a drop-off risk
3. A product release changes the experience at one or more journey stages
4. A new merchant segment is added that has a materially different journey from the Ideal Customer Profile
5. A new market is entered and localisation creates meaningful journey differences

Do not update the blueprint after every merchant complaint or every minor product change. Reserve updates for signals that represent a pattern across multiple merchants, not individual experiences.

---

### Principles for Improvement

**Fix drop-off before adding features.** If the wizard completion rate is below target, improving the wizard is a higher priority than building new features that non-converting merchants will never use.

**Test before committing.** Before a major journey change is made — a new onboarding sequence, a revised email template, a changed wizard step — define what success looks like and how it will be measured.

**Listen to the middle of the journey, not just the ends.** Most effort naturally goes to acquisition (top of funnel) and conversion (trial-to-paid). The merchant experience between day 7 and day 21 is often the least observed and the most impactful on conversion outcomes.

**Celebrate what is working.** A section of the journey that performs above expectation should be documented, understood, and used as a model for improving weaker stages. Not everything needs to change.
