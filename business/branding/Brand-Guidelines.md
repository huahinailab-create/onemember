## Executive Summary

OneMember helps small businesses keep customers coming back.

That is the entire business. Every product decision, every marketing message, every design choice, and every conversation with a merchant should connect back to that single idea.

OneMember is **not** selling loyalty software. OneMember is selling **customer retention** — a business outcome that every merchant in the world already understands and wants.

The merchants OneMember serves are busy people. They run cafés, salons, restaurants, pet shops, and retail stores. They are excellent at their craft. They do not want to learn technology. They do not have time for complicated tools. They want something simple that works and helps their business grow.

Everything OneMember produces — its product, its marketing, its support, its communications — must feel like it was made specifically for that person.

**If it is complicated, it is wrong.**
**If it is unclear, it is wrong.**
**If a merchant needs to think twice to understand it, it is wrong.**

This document defines the standards that prevent those mistakes from happening.

## Brand Mission

> **Empower independent merchants to build lasting customer loyalty — without the complexity or cost of enterprise software.**

OneMember exists because independent merchants are underserved. Enterprise loyalty platforms are built for large retail chains with IT departments and implementation budgets. Paper stamp cards generate no data and get lost. Nothing in between serves the café owner, the salon operator, the pet shop manager — the millions of small business owners who want to reward their regulars but cannot afford to make it complicated.

OneMember fills that gap.

## Brand Vision

OneMember will become the go-to customer retention platform for independent and small-chain merchants across Southeast Asia and beyond.

Every merchant, regardless of technical skill, will be able to launch a professional loyalty programme in minutes, keep more customers coming back, and grow their repeat business — all from a single, affordable tool.

## Brand Values

These values are not aspirational. They describe how OneMember already operates and must continue to operate as the company grows.

### Simplicity

Complexity is the enemy. Every feature, every piece of copy, every screen in the product must earn its place by being easy to understand. If a merchant cannot figure it out without help, it is not simple enough.

### Honesty

OneMember does not overpromise. The product does what it says it does. Pricing is transparent. Limitations are disclosed. Merchants are never surprised.

### Respect for the Merchant's Time

A merchant using OneMember is doing so between serving customers. Every interaction — from signing up to recording a purchase to reading an email — must be as fast and efficient as possible.

### Affordability

OneMember is priced for owner-operators, not IT departments. Value must be obvious from the first week. The product must be so clearly useful that the price feels like a small expense compared to the benefit it delivers.

### Trustworthiness

Member data is protected. Transaction history is accurate. Nothing disappears. When a merchant gives their customer data to OneMember, they are extending their trust. That trust must be earned every day.

## Target Audience

### Primary Market

**Thailand**

The initial development and launch market. Large independent merchant base. Growing digital adoption among SMEs. Merchants are accustomed to paper stamp cards and recognise the limitation of them.

### Secondary Markets

**Singapore** — High digital adoption; merchants comfortable with SaaS pricing; English-first market.

**Malaysia** — Large SME community; bilingual English and Malay market; strong F&B and retail sectors.

**UAE** — High-income market; growing SME ecosystem in Dubai and Abu Dhabi; appetite for modern SaaS tools.

### Priority Industries

OneMember is most relevant to businesses where customers return on a regular basis and where a tangible reason to return makes a measurable difference.

**Food & Beverage**
Cafés, coffee shops, restaurants, bubble tea shops, bakeries. High visit frequency. Daily and weekly regulars form the core revenue base. Loyalty programmes reinforce the habit of returning.

**Beauty & Wellness**
Hair salons, nail salons, massage and spa businesses, cosmetic shops. Regular appointment cycles. Clients have many choices. A loyalty reward creates a tangible reason to return to the same place.

**Health & Fitness**
Gyms, yoga studios, dental clinics, medical clinics. Clients build relationships with practitioners. A loyalty programme reinforces that relationship and rewards commitment.

**Retail**
Fashion boutiques, grocery stores, pet shops, specialty stores, wholesale businesses. Repeat purchase cycles. Loyalty programmes increase basket size and visit frequency.

**Services**
Car wash and car detailing, dry cleaners, tutoring centres. Regular maintenance cycles. Loyalty rewards accelerate the decision to return sooner.

## Ideal Merchant

OneMember is designed for one specific person.

**They are an owner-operator.** They work in their own business. They know their regulars by name. They care deeply about their reputation and their customers' experience.

**They are not technical.** They use a smartphone and a POS system, but they did not choose either based on specifications. They chose them because someone recommended them or because they were easy.

**They are busy.** They are behind the counter, managing staff, ordering stock, serving customers. They do not have time to learn complicated software or read long manuals.

**They are cost-conscious.** Every baht, ringgit, or dollar matters. They will not pay for something unless the value is obvious.

**They are currently using paper stamp cards** — or nothing at all. They know paper cards get lost. They know they cannot track who their best customers are. They know they are not doing enough to keep customers coming back. But they have not found anything simple enough to replace it.

**OneMember is built for them.**

## Tone of Voice

### The Voice in One Sentence

Speak to merchants the way a knowledgeable friend who runs a successful business would speak to them. Direct, warm, practical, and always on their side.

### What This Sounds Like

**Direct** — Get to the point. Merchants are busy. Say what matters first.

**Warm** — Friendly and encouraging, not formal or cold. Use "you" and "your business" rather than "the merchant" or "the user."

**Practical** — Talk about what actually happens, not what could theoretically happen. Specific outcomes beat vague promises.

**Confident** — OneMember knows loyalty. Give clear recommendations. Lead with the answer, not with caveats.

**Plain** — Use the simplest word that does the job. If a merchant who finished secondary school would not immediately understand it, rewrite it.

### What This Does NOT Sound Like

**Corporate** — "We leverage our proprietary loyalty infrastructure to drive customer engagement and maximize lifetime value."

**Cold** — "Your subscription will be terminated at the end of the trial period if payment is not received."

**Technical** — "OneMember's API-first architecture enables seamless POS integration via webhook callbacks."

**Aggressive** — "Don't lose another customer! Act now or fall behind your competitors!"

**Patronising** — "Our platform is so simple, even technophobes can use it!"

---

## Writing Style

### The Core Rules

**Lead with the benefit, not the feature.**
Merchants do not want software. They want results. Tell them what they get, not how the software works.

**Short sentences.**
One idea per sentence. If a sentence has more than three clauses, break it up.

**Plain words.**
Use words a busy shop owner would use in conversation. If a simpler word exists, use it.

**Explain why before how.**
Before explaining how something works, tell the merchant why it matters to their business.

**Active voice.**
"OneMember records every purchase" not "Every purchase is recorded by OneMember."

**No filler.**
Every sentence must earn its place. Remove any sentence that could be deleted without losing meaning.

---

### Good Examples

> "Your customers already visited once. OneMember helps bring them back."

> "Set up your loyalty programme today. It takes 10 minutes."

> "See who your best customers are and reward them."

> "No app download needed. No developer required. No contract."

> "Replace your paper stamp cards with something your customers will actually use."

---

### Bad Examples

> "Leverage our advanced loyalty infrastructure to drive customer engagement metrics."

→ **Why it's wrong:** Jargon-heavy. No merchant cares about "infrastructure" or "metrics." Says nothing about what they get.

> "OneMember's comprehensive loyalty management platform facilitates the configuration and deployment of multi-tier reward structures."

→ **Why it's wrong:** Sounds like enterprise software. Uses five words where one would do. No benefit stated.

> "In order to utilise the full functionality of the system, merchants are advised to complete all onboarding steps prior to adding member records."

→ **Why it's wrong:** Passive, bureaucratic, cold. Would make any merchant feel like they are reading a government form.

> "Don't let your competitors steal your customers! Upgrade now before it's too late!"

→ **Why it's wrong:** Aggressive and fear-driven. This is not how OneMember speaks.

## Messaging Principles

Every piece of communication — an ad, a web page, an email, a product notification — should reflect at least one of these principles.

### 1. Customers Already Exist

The most powerful message OneMember can deliver is this: merchants already have customers. Those customers visited once. OneMember helps bring them back. This is always the starting point.

*"Your customers already came once. OneMember helps them come back."*

### 2. Retention Is Cheaper Than Acquisition

Keeping an existing customer is easier and cheaper than finding a new one. This is a business truth that resonates with any merchant who has ever spent money on advertising with uncertain results.

*"More repeat visits. Less marketing spend."*

### 3. Simple Is the Promise

OneMember's competitive advantage begins with simplicity. Any message that suggests complexity undermines the brand. Keep it simple — in what is said and how it is said.

*"Set up today. It takes 10 minutes."*

### 4. Paper Cards Are Broken

Most merchants who would benefit from OneMember are either using paper stamp cards or nothing at all. Paper cards get lost, generate no data, and cannot tell a merchant who their best customers are. This is a relatable pain point.

*"Replace the paper card your customers keep losing."*

### 5. Merchants Can Start Today

The 30-day free trial with no credit card required means there is no barrier to starting. Every message should make this feel easy and immediate.

*"Start free. No credit card needed."*

### 6. Outcomes, Not Features

OneMember is selling a business outcome — more repeat customers, more revenue, stronger relationships. Features enable that outcome, but they should never lead a message.

| Don't lead with the feature... | Lead with the outcome |
|-------------------------------|----------------------|
| "Points-based loyalty programmes" | "Keep customers coming back with every visit" |
| "Member management dashboard" | "Know who your best customers are" |
| "Reward redemption tracking" | "See every reward your customers have earned" |

## Visual Identity Principles

These principles provide strategic direction for designers and marketers. Specific colour values, typeface licenses, and logo files are managed separately. No specific HEX values are defined in this document.

### Colour Philosophy

**Primary palette: Clean, trustworthy, modern**

OneMember's colour choices should communicate professionalism and simplicity. The dominant impression should be clean, open, and inviting — the visual equivalent of a well-organised, friendly business.

Use colour with purpose. One primary brand colour should anchor all communications. Supporting colours should assist readability and direct attention, not compete with content.

**Warmth is welcome.** Unlike enterprise software which tends toward cold blues and greys, OneMember can carry warmth. Warm accents reflect the independent business environments OneMember serves — cafés, salons, restaurants — without becoming casual or unpolished.

**Avoid** dark, heavy, or intimidating colour schemes. OneMember should feel approachable, not corporate.

---

### Typography Philosophy

**Clarity over decoration.**

Body text must be easy to read at small sizes on any screen — a merchant may be reading on an ageing phone in a bright shop environment. Use a clean, modern sans-serif typeface that performs well across devices and resolutions.

**Hierarchy matters.** Headlines should be visually distinct from body text. Labels, captions, and supporting text should be clearly differentiated. The merchant should never have to search for what is most important on a page.

**Keep it concise.** Typography choices should enforce brevity. If a paragraph is too long, the design has failed before the writing has. Line length, leading, and spacing should support short, scannable content.

---

### Photography Style

**Real. Human. Local.**

Photography in OneMember's marketing should feel authentic to the merchants it serves. Images should feature real business environments — counters, cafés, salons, shop floors — with real people at work and with their customers.

**Warm lighting.** Bright and inviting, not clinical or dramatic. The environments in these photos should feel like places the merchant is proud of.

**Southeast Asian context.** For the primary market, images should reflect Thai and Southeast Asian merchant environments. People, settings, and products should feel familiar and relevant to the target audience.

**What to avoid:**
- Generic stock photography of suited professionals in glass offices
- Overly staged or artificial setups
- Western-only contexts when marketing to Southeast Asian merchants
- Technology-first imagery (laptops, dashboards, data visualisations) as the hero

---

### Illustration Style

**Simple. Friendly. Purposeful.**

If illustration is used, it should reinforce simplicity — not add complexity. Use line-based or flat illustrations to explain concepts that are difficult to photograph, such as the flow of points from purchase to reward.

Illustration should never be the primary communication vehicle. Use it to support written content, not replace it.

Avoid overly playful or cartoon-like illustration styles that would undermine OneMember's professional credibility with business owners.

---

### Iconography

Icons should be simple and immediately recognisable. Use a consistent icon library throughout. Do not mix styles from different icon sets.

Icons should reinforce meaning, not replace words. In contexts where merchants may be unfamiliar with an icon, always pair it with a label.

**Standard usage:**
- Navigation icons to aid scanning (matching what the product already uses)
- Status icons for alerts, success states, and warnings
- Category icons to distinguish sections within longer content

---

### Whitespace

**Use generous whitespace.**

Whitespace is not empty space — it is clarity. Generous spacing between elements makes content easier to read and the design feel more professional. Cramped layouts suggest a product trying to fit too much in, which conflicts with the brand's promise of simplicity.

In marketing materials: let messages breathe. A single strong headline with space around it is more powerful than four messages competing for attention.

In the product: clear visual separation between sections reduces cognitive load and helps merchants find what they need faster.

---

### Modern SaaS Inspiration

OneMember should look like it belongs alongside the best modern SaaS products available today. Clean cards. Clear hierarchy. Subtle shadows and borders. Fast, responsive interfaces. Consistent spacing systems.

Think of the kind of software that wins design awards for being clean, not the kind that wins for being visually spectacular. OneMember does not need to impress designers — it needs to help merchants do their jobs.

**Principles to follow:**
- Content-first layouts — the data, not the chrome, is the hero
- Responsive by default — looks right on a desktop browser, a tablet, and a mobile phone
- Minimal decoration — no gradients, shadows, or effects that do not add clarity
- Consistent components — the same button, the same card, the same form always looks the same

## User Experience Principles

The product must embody the brand promise in every interaction.

### Simple

Every screen has one primary job. A merchant looking at any page in OneMember should immediately know what they are supposed to do next. When there are choices, the recommended choice is obvious. Complexity is hidden behind good defaults.

### Fast

Every action should take as few steps as possible. Merchants should be able to add a member, record a purchase, or check their top customers in under 30 seconds. Speed is a feature.

### Friendly

The product should be encouraging. Error messages should explain the problem and suggest the fix — not just tell the merchant something went wrong. Onboarding should celebrate progress. Empty states should explain what to do next, not just display an empty screen.

### Forgiving

Mistakes happen. The product should make it easy to correct them. Where actions are permanent or significant, the merchant should be asked to confirm. Where possible, provide undo or edit options.

### Accessible

Merchants may be using OneMember on older devices, in bright lighting, or with varying levels of digital literacy. Text must be readable. Contrast must be sufficient. Actions must be clear. The product must not require expertise in software to use.

### Minimal Clicks

Every unnecessary click is a friction point that increases the chance a merchant abandons the task. Count the steps in every flow. If there is a way to reduce the number of clicks to complete a task, take it.

## Marketing Principles

### Every Advertisement Should Communicate

1. **The benefit** — what the merchant gets, not what the product does
2. **The simplicity** — that it is easy to start and easy to use
3. **The relevance** — that it is built for businesses like theirs
4. **The call to action** — what they should do next (always specific, never vague)

Every advertisement should pass this test: could a busy café owner understand it in the time it takes their kettle to boil?

---

### Every Landing Page Should Communicate

1. **What OneMember does** — in the first sentence, without jargon
2. **Who it is for** — merchants should immediately recognise themselves in the description
3. **Why it is different** — simple to set up, affordable, designed for their business
4. **What happens next** — a clear, low-commitment call to action (Start Free, Try for 30 Days)
5. **That there is no risk** — free trial, no credit card required, no contract

Landing pages should never require scrolling to understand whether OneMember is relevant. The most important message belongs at the top of the page, above the fold, in plain language.

---

### Every Email Should Communicate

1. **One idea** — emails with multiple goals have no goal
2. **A clear benefit** — what the merchant will gain by reading or acting
3. **A single call to action** — one link, one button, one next step
4. **Brevity** — if it cannot be read in 60 seconds, it is too long for this audience

Subject lines should be specific and plain. The subject line is the ad for the email. It must give the merchant a reason to open, without being misleading or manipulative.

**Examples:**
- "Your loyalty programme is ready. Here's your first step."
- "3 ways to get your first 10 members this week."
- "A customer redeemed a reward today. Here's how to see it."

**Not:**
- "Exciting news from the OneMember team!"
- "Don't miss out on this opportunity to maximise your customer engagement!"

## Sales Principles

### How OneMember Should Be Presented

OneMember should be presented as a business tool that helps merchants earn more from the customers they already have.

**Start with the problem.** Ask the merchant how they currently keep their regulars coming back. Most will say they do not have a system. That is the opening.

**Connect to their business.** Use the merchant's own industry and context. "For a café like yours, most regulars come in three or four times a week. With OneMember, they are earning rewards every visit. That gives them a reason to choose you over the café down the street."

**Show the simplicity.** The fastest path to a yes is showing the merchant that this is not complicated. The onboarding wizard takes 10 minutes. Adding a member takes 30 seconds. Recording a purchase takes 15 seconds. Demonstrate this if possible.

**The trial removes all risk.** Every merchant gets 30 days free with full access. No credit card required. If they do not like it, they have lost nothing. This removes the most common objection — "I am not sure if it is worth the money."

---

### What Should Never Be Promised

- Guaranteed increases in revenue or customer frequency — OneMember provides the tool; the results depend on how the merchant uses it
- Features that are not yet built — if a merchant asks about something on the roadmap, be honest about its status
- Pricing that has not been confirmed — always direct to the current pricing page
- Integration with specific POS systems unless confirmed — say "we can explore this" not "yes, we integrate with that"
- That OneMember replaces a POS, a CRM, or an accounting tool — it does not

## Brand Do's

**Do** lead with business outcomes. "More regulars" not "loyalty software."

**Do** use short sentences. One idea per sentence.

**Do** speak directly to the merchant. "You" not "merchants" or "users."

**Do** celebrate simplicity. "Set up in 10 minutes" is a feature worth repeating.

**Do** remove jargon before publishing anything. If you needed to explain a word, replace it.

**Do** address the paper stamp card. It is what most OneMember merchants are replacing.

**Do** make the free trial the hero of the sales process. No risk means no excuse to wait.

**Do** test every message against this question: Would a busy shop owner understand this in five seconds?

**Do** use real, specific language. "10 minutes" not "minutes." "30 days free" not "a free trial."

**Do** keep visual design clean and uncluttered. More whitespace, fewer words, fewer elements.

---

## Brand Don'ts

**Don't** use the word "solution." It is the most common piece of enterprise jargon. OneMember is a tool, a platform, or a loyalty programme — never a "solution."

**Don't** use "leverage," "synergy," "scalable," "robust," "seamless," "holistic," or any word more at home in a boardroom than a café.

**Don't** talk about features before outcomes. Nobody wants to buy a loyalty programme. They want more regular customers.

**Don't** make merchants feel they are missing something if they do not upgrade immediately. The free trial exists to build trust, not to manufacture urgency.

**Don't** design anything that looks complicated. If a screen, a flyer, or an email looks like it requires effort to understand, start again.

**Don't** use fear-based marketing. "Your customers are leaving" and "your competitors are winning" are not OneMember's voice. The tone is optimistic, not threatening.

**Don't** make claims that cannot be substantiated. No invented statistics, no fabricated results, no "customers see 40% more repeat visits" unless the data to support that statement exists.

**Don't** position OneMember as an enterprise product. The moment OneMember starts sounding like enterprise software, it has lost the merchant it was built for.

**Don't** over-explain. A merchant who has to read a paragraph to understand what OneMember does has already lost interest.

**Don't** forget the secondary markets. In communications for Singapore, Malaysia, and UAE audiences, use the local currency, local business context, and relevant examples for those markets.
