## 1. Executive Summary

### Purpose

This playbook is the single source of truth for how OneMember is sold. It documents the philosophy, process, language, and techniques that allow any person — founder, salesperson, business development partner, or country distributor — to sell OneMember consistently and in alignment with the brand.

It is a living document. When sales conversations surface new objections, better discovery questions, or more effective ways to explain value, this playbook should be updated to reflect that learning.

---

### Sales Philosophy

OneMember is sold through **consultative selling**.

This means we begin with the merchant's problem — not our product. We listen before we speak. We ask questions before we demonstrate. We recommend only when we believe OneMember is genuinely the right solution for this merchant.

We do not pressure. We do not create false urgency. We do not exaggerate what OneMember does.

We help merchants discover whether and how OneMember solves a business problem they actually have. If it does, we make the path to starting as easy as possible. If it does not, we say so honestly.

This philosophy is not idealistic. It is strategic. Independent merchants in Thailand are a relationship-driven market. A merchant who starts OneMember because they understood its value — not because they were sold to — becomes a long-term customer and a source of referrals. A merchant who starts because of pressure becomes a churn risk and a reputational risk.

**Long-term trust is our most important competitive advantage.**

---

### How OneMember Should Be Sold

Three principles govern every sales conversation:

**1. Sell retention, not software.**
OneMember is not a loyalty programme tool. OneMember helps businesses keep customers coming back. Frame every conversation around that outcome. Features are evidence. Business results are the point.

**2. Make the merchant the expert on their business.**
We do not tell merchants what they need. We ask questions and let them articulate their own problem. When a merchant describes their situation in their own words, they become their own salesperson. Our job is to create the conditions for that conversation.

**3. Remove risk from every decision.**
The free 30-day trial with no credit card required is our most important sales tool. Every conversation should end with an easy, risk-free next step. We are never asking for a commitment. We are asking for a start.

## 2. Ideal Customer Profile

### Best-Fit Industries

The following industries represent the highest probability of success for OneMember. They share three common characteristics: repeat customer behaviour is core to revenue, the owner-operator is the decision-maker, and a loyalty programme solves a problem the merchant already recognises.

| Industry | Why They Are a Great Fit |
|----------|------------------------|
| **Coffee Shops & Cafés** | Daily or near-daily customer habit; existing paper stamp card culture; extremely fast to see value |
| **Hair Salons** | Strong stylist-client loyalty; 4–6 week visit cycle creates natural stamp card rhythm |
| **Beauty & Aesthetic Clinics** | High transaction value; points-based rewards feel premium; strong client retention motivation |
| **Restaurants** | Repeat diners are the revenue core; birthday rewards are highly valued |
| **Pet Grooming Salons** | Pet owners are intensely loyal once a relationship is established; birthday reward for the pet is uniquely powerful |
| **Gyms & Fitness Studios** | Retention is a primary business problem; consistent members build toward rewards |
| **Independent Retail** | Owner wants to move away from discount cards that erode margin |
| **Dental Clinics** | Routine appointment frequency; points motivate proactive appointment booking |
| **Bakeries & Specialty Food** | Community-oriented; regulars are the foundation; morning traffic is highly repeatable |
| **Car Washes** | Stamp card mentality already exists; digital replaces paper seamlessly |

---

### Best Merchant Size

| Dimension | Ideal Profile |
|-----------|--------------|
| **Locations** | 1–3 (single location is ideal) |
| **Staff** | 1–10 |
| **Decision-maker** | Owner or co-owner present on premises |
| **Technical comfort** | Can use a smartphone or tablet; no developer knowledge required |
| **Current loyalty** | Paper stamp cards, nothing at all, or a spreadsheet |
| **Customer base** | Primarily repeat customers with regulars forming a visible group |
| **Awareness of retention** | The merchant recognises that regulars matter — even if they don't use the word "retention" |

---

### Buying Signals

These are situations or statements that indicate a merchant is ready to evaluate OneMember now.

**Situational signals:**
- A new competitor has recently opened nearby
- They recently lost a regular customer or noticed a drop in repeat visits
- They are preparing to launch or reopen a business
- They are replacing or upgrading other business software
- They are attending a trade show, business event, or industry association meeting
- A trusted peer has recently mentioned loyalty programmes or OneMember

**Verbal signals from the merchant:**
- "My regulars keep losing their stamp cards."
- "I know who my regulars are but there's no way to track them."
- "I want to do something for my loyal customers."
- "I've been thinking about doing a loyalty programme."
- "How do I get people to come back more often?"
- "My competitor has a points programme — should we do something like that?"
- "We spend a lot on getting new customers but our regulars don't feel recognised."

---

### Poor-Fit Prospects

Not every business is a good fit. Recognising a poor fit early saves time for both the salesperson and the merchant.

| Signal | Why It's a Poor Fit |
|--------|---------------------|
| Primarily walk-in or tourist traffic | Repeat customer behaviour is not central to revenue; loyalty programme value is reduced |
| No existing base of regulars | A loyalty programme enhances existing repeat behaviour — it does not create it from scratch |
| Single transaction per customer lifetime (e.g., real estate, wedding venues) | Loyalty programmes require repeat visits to deliver value |
| Very high staff turnover or inconsistent operations | Programme success depends on consistent execution at the counter |
| No staff at the counter to add members | Someone must be there to enrol members at the point of service |
| Owner is not engaged in the business day-to-day | Without owner involvement, adoption is low |
| Actively looking for an enterprise feature set | If the merchant requires API integrations, custom workflows, or multi-location management today, OneMember V1.0 is not the right tool |

---

### When to Politely Walk Away

It is acceptable — and professional — to tell a merchant that OneMember is not right for them today.

Walk away when:

- The merchant's core business does not depend on repeat customers
- The merchant wants features OneMember does not have and will not have soon
- The merchant is not the decision-maker and the real decision-maker is unavailable to evaluate the product
- The merchant is only interested in a free or heavily discounted deal with no intention of paying
- The merchant is skeptical to the point where no amount of explanation will move them — some merchants will change their minds on their own timeline; continuing to push damages the relationship

When walking away: be warm, be brief, leave the door open.

> "I don't think OneMember is the right fit for where your business is right now — but if that changes, the trial is always available and you can start any time. It was great to meet you."

That sentence costs nothing and preserves the relationship for the future.

## 3. Sales Process

OneMember's sales process has ten stages. Every lead should be trackable to a stage. Progress between stages requires specific criteria to be met.

---

### Stage 1 — Lead

**Definition:** A business or individual has been identified as a potential OneMember merchant, but no contact has been made or no inbound enquiry has been received.

**Sources:** Cold outreach (walk-in, phone, LinkedIn), referral from existing merchant, partner referral, inbound website sign-up, event meeting, social media enquiry.

**Sales objective:** Determine if there is enough fit to invest time in qualification.

**Exit criteria:** Initial contact made; merchant has agreed to a conversation, or has self-qualified by signing up for a free trial.

---

### Stage 2 — Qualification

**Definition:** Initial contact has been made. The purpose of this stage is to determine whether the merchant is worth pursuing and whether OneMember is likely to be a genuine fit.

**Sales objective:** Confirm that the merchant has repeat customers, a business problem OneMember can solve, and a decision-maker engaged in the conversation. This is NOT a demo.

**Duration:** 5–10 minutes (phone, visit, or message exchange).

**Key questions at this stage:**
- What type of business do you have?
- Do you have regular customers who come back often?
- Have you ever thought about or tried a loyalty programme?

**Exit criteria (qualified):** Merchant operates a repeat-customer business, is the decision-maker or can involve the decision-maker, and has expressed interest in a discovery conversation or demo.

**Exit criteria (disqualified):** Merchant does not have a repeat customer base, is not the decision-maker, or shows clear signs of poor fit (see Section 2).

---

### Stage 3 — Discovery

**Definition:** A structured conversation designed to understand the merchant's business, customers, challenges, and goals — before showing any product.

**Sales objective:** Understand the merchant well enough to demonstrate OneMember specifically in the context of their business, not generically. Build trust by listening first.

**Duration:** 15–30 minutes.

**Format:** In person, video call, or phone. Always in person if possible for a new merchant.

**Key principle:** This is a conversation, not a checklist. Use the discovery questions in Section 4. Let the merchant talk. Ask follow-up questions. Take notes. Reference what they said later in the demo.

**Exit criteria:** You understand enough about the merchant's business, customers, current loyalty approach, and challenges to conduct a relevant demo. The merchant has agreed to a demo.

---

### Stage 4 — Demo

**Definition:** A guided walkthrough of OneMember, personalised to the merchant's specific situation based on discovery.

**Sales objective:** Show the merchant how OneMember would work for their specific business. Connect every feature to an outcome the merchant mentioned in discovery. End with a clear, easy next step: start the free trial.

**Duration:** 10–15 minutes (product demo) + 5–10 minutes (questions and next steps).

**Format:** Live on a device, in person if possible. Screen share if remote.

**Exit criteria:** Merchant has started a free trial, agreed to a specific date to start, or has a specific, concrete reason for not starting yet (which generates a follow-up task).

See Section 6 for the complete Demo Playbook.

---

### Stage 5 — Trial

**Definition:** The merchant has signed up for the 30-day free trial. The trial period is active.

**Sales objective:** Help the merchant activate — specifically: complete setup, add first member, record first purchase, and reach the first reward redemption. Activation is the single biggest predictor of conversion to paid.

**Duration:** 30 days.

**Key principle:** Do not disappear during the trial. This is not a "set it and forget it" stage. Regular, light-touch contact at specific intervals makes a measurable difference to activation and conversion rates.

**Exit criteria:** Merchant has reached "activated" status (first member + first purchase recorded) AND is on a clear path to the first redemption.

See Section 9 for the complete Trial Success Playbook.

---

### Stage 6 — Follow-Up

**Definition:** Ongoing contact throughout the trial period and at key milestones.

**Sales objective:** Maintain the relationship, resolve friction, reinforce value, and prepare the merchant for the upgrade conversation.

**Key principle:** Contact should be helpful — not sales-driven. Ask how it's going. Offer tips. Share a relevant observation. Every message should give value, not just ask for something.

**Exit criteria:** Trial is progressing toward activation; merchant is responsive; upgrade conversation has been introduced.

---

### Stage 7 — Trial Success

**Definition:** The merchant has reached activation (first member added, first purchase recorded, first reward redemption processed). They have direct evidence that the loyalty programme works.

**Sales objective:** Capture the merchant's momentum and convert it to a paid subscription. This is the highest-probability close moment. The merchant has seen the product work with a real customer.

**Exit criteria:** Merchant has been presented with plan options and has either upgraded or has a concrete decision timeline.

---

### Stage 8 — Upgrade

**Definition:** The merchant has converted from free trial to a paid subscription.

**Sales objective:** Confirm the merchant is set up for success. Reinforce that they made the right decision. Introduce them to any features or capabilities they have not yet used.

**Exit criteria:** Subscription is confirmed; merchant is active and logging in regularly.

---

### Stage 9 — Referral

**Definition:** The merchant is active, satisfied, and has been in contact with the OneMember team at least once since upgrading.

**Sales objective:** Ask for a referral in a natural, non-pressured way. A satisfied merchant is the best source of new merchants.

**Exit criteria:** Merchant has either provided a referral or has been asked and the timing noted for a follow-up request.

See Section 11 for the complete Referral Strategy.

---

### Stage 10 — Expansion

**Definition:** The merchant is a paying subscriber with a well-established programme.

**Sales objective:** Identify opportunities to expand the merchant's use of OneMember — higher plan tier, multi-location (when available), additional campaigns, or features they have not used.

**Key principle:** Expansion is driven by the merchant's needs, not by revenue targets. Recommend an upgrade only when it genuinely solves a problem the merchant has. Premature upsells damage trust.

**Exit criteria:** Merchant is on the appropriate plan for their current stage and is aware of the options available if their needs grow.

## 4. Discovery Questions

Discovery questions should feel like genuine curiosity — because they are. These are questions a knowledgeable business advisor would ask, not a sales representative running through a script. Ask in the order that makes sense for the conversation. Skip questions that have already been answered naturally. Follow up on anything interesting.

---

### Business Questions

1. How long have you been running this business?
2. How did the business get started?
3. How many locations do you operate?
4. How many staff do you have working at any given time?
5. Who manages the front-of-house or counter day-to-day — is that primarily you?
6. What does a typical busy day look like for you?
7. What are your peak hours or days?
8. What is the average purchase amount per customer visit?
9. How many customers would you estimate come through on a typical day?
10. Have you thought about how much of your revenue comes from regulars versus new customers?

---

### Customer Questions

11. Would you say you have a strong base of regular customers?
12. Do you recognise many of your customers by name or face?
13. How often do your best regulars tend to come back?
14. Can you describe your typical regular customer in a sentence or two?
15. Are there types of customers you get a lot of that you wish would come in more often?
16. Have you noticed any patterns in who tends to stay loyal versus who comes once and disappears?
17. How do customers find you for the first time?
18. When a regular customer stops coming, do you usually know why?
19. Have you ever tried to win back a customer who stopped visiting?
20. What do you think makes your regulars choose you over other options nearby?

---

### Current Loyalty Questions

21. Are you currently running any kind of loyalty programme or reward for regular customers?
22. If yes — tell me about it. How does it work? How long have you been running it?
23. Do you use paper stamp cards at the moment?
24. How often do customers actually use them and complete the card?
25. Have you tried any digital loyalty apps or software before?
26. If yes — what was that experience like? What did you like or dislike about it?
27. Do you have a way to keep track of who your regular customers are?
28. Do you collect any contact information from your customers today?
29. Do you know who your top 10 customers are by visit frequency or spend?
30. Have you tried any discount-based loyalty programmes — where regulars get a percentage off?

---

### Challenges Questions

31. What is your biggest challenge when it comes to keeping customers coming back?
32. Have you ever lost a regular customer to a competitor? What happened?
33. Is there anything about your current approach to customer retention that frustrates you?
34. Are there quiet periods in your week or month where you wish your regulars were coming in more?
35. Do you find it hard to recognise and reward customers who are genuinely loyal to you?
36. Is there anything about the paper stamp card that doesn't work well for you?
37. What happens when a customer loses their stamp card — do you replace their progress?
38. Is staff consistency an issue — do different team members handle customers differently?
39. Have you ever wanted to do something special for your best customers and not had a way to?
40. Do you have a sense of how much a loyal regular is worth to your business over a year?

---

### Revenue Questions

41. Do you have a rough sense of what percentage of your revenue comes from repeat customers?
42. Would you say your business is growing, stable, or facing some pressure right now?
43. Are there quieter months or seasons that you'd like to smooth out?
44. Have you experimented with promotions or discounts to drive more visits? How did that go?
45. If you could bring back just one more regular per week who had drifted away, what would that be worth to your business?

---

### Marketing Questions

46. What does your current marketing look like — social media, local ads, word of mouth?
47. Where do you find the most effective way to attract new customers?
48. Have you done any email or SMS marketing to your customer base?
49. What is your feeling about social media for your type of business?
50. Are you spending more on attracting new customers or keeping the ones you have?

---

### Growth Questions

51. Where do you want this business to be in two years?
52. Are you planning to open additional locations at any point?
53. What would need to change for you to feel confident the business was growing?
54. Are there any types of customers or occasions you'd like to attract more of?
55. If you could solve one business problem this quarter, what would it be?

---

### Staff Questions

56. How many staff are involved in serving customers at the counter?
57. Do you think your staff would be able to learn a new system quickly?
58. How do you currently train new staff on customer interactions?
59. Is there one person on your team who tends to be the most tech-comfortable?
60. Would you need to be the person who sets it up, or could someone on your team do that?

---

### Technology Questions

61. What devices do you have available at the counter — phone, tablet, computer?
62. Are you comfortable with web-based software — things that run in a browser, not an installed app?
63. Have you found it difficult to get your staff to use new technology in the past?
64. What software or apps do you currently use in your business?
65. Do you use a POS system? Which one?

---

### Goals Questions

66. What does success look like for your loyalty programme in six months?
67. If a loyalty programme did one thing for you, what would you want it to be?
68. What would make you feel confident that the investment was worth it?
69. Are there specific occasions — birthdays, anniversaries, milestones — that you'd like to use to reconnect with customers?
70. How important is it to you to know who your most loyal customers are, by name?

## 5. Qualification Framework

Use this framework after the discovery conversation to score each prospect and prioritise pipeline accordingly. This is a guide for judgment — not a mechanical formula.

---

### Scoring Model

Score each dimension from 1 (low fit) to 3 (strong fit). Maximum score: 24 points.

| Dimension | 1 — Low Fit | 2 — Some Fit | 3 — Strong Fit |
|-----------|------------|-------------|---------------|
| **Need** | No clear retention problem; satisfied with status quo | Has a problem but hasn't prioritised solving it | Actively experiencing lost regulars or has a clear retention gap |
| **Budget** | Unwilling to consider any spend | Open to spending but price-sensitive | Understands cost of loyalty programme relative to business value |
| **Decision-maker** | Not the decision-maker; real decision-maker is unavailable | Is part of the decision but needs sign-off from partner | Is the sole decision-maker; can say yes today |
| **Timeline** | No urgency; looking for something "at some point" | Planning to do something in the next few months | Has a specific event or date creating urgency (relaunch, competition, new season) |
| **Business fit** | Primarily one-time or tourist customers | Mix of repeat and one-time customers | Regulars are clearly the core of the business |
| **Growth potential** | Declining business, no growth ambition | Stable; open to modest growth | Clear growth mindset; loyalty programme fits their trajectory |
| **Support effort** | Will require significant hand-holding to set up and use consistently | Will need some guidance but is capable of self-service | Technically comfortable; can set up and use independently |
| **Referral potential** | Isolated; no visible community or peer network | Some connections in the industry | Well-connected; knows other business owners; active in business associations |

---

### Score Interpretation

| Score | Interpretation | Recommended Action |
|-------|---------------|-------------------|
| **20–24** | Ideal prospect | Prioritise. Move to demo immediately. High probability of trial activation and paid conversion. |
| **14–19** | Good prospect | Worth pursuing. Identify which dimensions scored low and address them. Likely converts given time and follow-up. |
| **8–13** | Marginal prospect | Proceed with caution. Identify what would need to change for fit to improve. Consider a lighter-touch approach. |
| **7 or below** | Poor fit | Do not invest significant time. Move to passive nurture or politely disengage. |

---

### Qualification Notes

Record the following in your CRM after every discovery conversation:

- Score (total and per dimension)
- Key pain expressed in the merchant's own words
- Best industry-specific talking point for this merchant
- Main objection or hesitation raised
- Decision-maker confirmed (Y/N)
- Specific next step agreed and date

## 6. Demo Playbook

### Overview

The demo is not a product tour. It is a business conversation that uses the product as evidence.

Every element of the demo should connect to something the merchant said during discovery. If you cannot connect a feature to the merchant's specific situation, do not show it. Less is more. A demo that shows five relevant things is more effective than one that shows fifteen features.

**Target duration:** 10–15 minutes of product walkthrough, followed by 5–10 minutes of questions and next steps.

---

### Preparation (Before the Demo)

Before any demo, confirm:

- [ ] You have completed a discovery conversation and have notes
- [ ] You know the merchant's industry and can speak their language (see Section 7)
- [ ] You know their current loyalty approach (paper cards, nothing, prior software)
- [ ] You know the main pain point in the merchant's own words
- [ ] You have a device ready with OneMember open — ideally logged in to a demo account, or your own account
- [ ] If in person: the merchant has a few minutes free from serving customers
- [ ] You know what you are NOT going to show — keep the demo focused

---

### Opening (2 minutes)

Start by referencing what you heard in discovery. This proves you listened and immediately personalises the demo.

**Script:**
> "Based on what you told me — [reference specific pain point in their words] — I want to show you exactly how OneMember would handle that. I'll keep it focused on what's most relevant to your business and skip anything that isn't. This will take about 10 minutes."

Do not start with "So, OneMember is a loyalty platform that..." Start with their problem.

---

### Opening Questions (Before Touching the Product)

Even if you completed discovery, ask one clarifying question before showing anything. It keeps the conversation two-directional and ensures you start with the right focus.

> "Before I show you anything — what's the one thing you most want to understand from today?"

Listen to the answer. Adjust the demo focus accordingly.

---

### Product Walkthrough — Sequence and Rationale

The following is the recommended demo sequence. Each step is followed by the talking point that connects it to business outcomes.

**Step 1 — The Dashboard (90 seconds)**

Show the dashboard first. It gives the merchant an immediate visual of what their business would look like with OneMember running.

> "This is what you'd see every day when you log in. Active members, purchases recorded today, rewards redeemed, your top customers. You can see at a glance whether your loyalty programme is doing its job — without needing to run any reports."

Point at one number (e.g., Top Members). Ask:
> "Do you know today who your top 10 customers are by how often they visit?"

Most merchants say no. That moment creates immediate relevance.

---

**Step 2 — Adding a Member (90 seconds)**

Navigate to Members → Add Member. Walk through the form.

> "This is how your team adds a customer. Name, phone number, optional birthday and notes. That's it. In our experience this takes about 30 seconds — fast enough to do while the customer is paying or collecting their order."

If the merchant has notes from discovery about losing paper cards, say:
> "And you mentioned customers losing their stamp cards — once someone is in the system, their progress is here. They never need a card. They just give their name."

---

**Step 3 — Recording a Purchase (90 seconds)**

Show the member profile. Demonstrate recording a purchase.

> "Once they're a member, recording a purchase takes about 15 seconds. Your team enters the amount — the points or stamps update instantly. The customer can see their progress. No manual calculation. No card to mark."

---

**Step 4 — The Campaign (2 minutes)**

Navigate to Campaigns. Show the active campaign — stamps or points, depending on what is most relevant for this merchant based on discovery.

> "This is the campaign — it's the engine behind the loyalty programme. It determines how customers earn rewards and what they get when they do. You set this up once and it runs automatically."

Explain the relevant model:
- For stamps: "Every visit earns a stamp. When they reach 10 — or whatever number you set — they earn a reward."
- For points: "Every 100 baht spent earns a point. When they reach 500 points, they get a reward you design."

> "You design the reward. Some businesses offer a free item. Some offer a discount. Some offer an upgrade. You decide what makes sense for your customers and your margins."

---

**Step 5 — Member Profile and Notes (60 seconds)**

Return to a member profile. Show the notes field and birthday field.

> "Each member has a profile with their full history — every purchase, every stamp, every redemption. And you can add private notes. Things only your team can see — their preferences, anything they mentioned, dietary needs, the name of their dog if you're a pet groomer."

If discovery revealed the merchant values personal relationships:
> "You mentioned that you know your regulars by name. OneMember lets you formalise that — it doesn't replace the personal relationship, it supports it."

---

**Step 6 — Birthday Reward (60 seconds)**

If the merchant's industry is one where birthday rewards are particularly relevant (salons, restaurants, clinics, gyms), briefly highlight this.

> "When you've added a member's birthday, OneMember reminds you when it's coming up. You can issue a reward. It sounds simple — but a customer who gets a birthday reward from your business remembers it. That's the kind of personal touch that makes a casual customer into a loyal one."

---

**Step 7 — Setup Time Confirmation (30 seconds)**

Close the walkthrough by anchoring the simplicity promise.

> "Everything you just saw — the campaign, the members, the rewards — took you 10 minutes to set up when you first joined. After that, your team does two things: add a member when someone new comes in, and record a purchase when they buy something. That's the whole programme."

---

### Merchant Interaction During the Demo

Do not monologue. After every step, ask a short question to confirm relevance:

- "Does this match what you were imagining?"
- "Is this the kind of thing you'd want your team to do?"
- "Does the stamp model or the points model feel more natural for your business?"
- "Can you see this working at your counter?"

If the merchant asks a question mid-demo, answer it and then ask: "Is that what you were asking?" before continuing.

---

### What NOT to Show

Unless the merchant specifically asks about them, skip:

- Settings and configuration options (too detailed, not relevant to outcomes)
- Multiple campaigns (one clear example is enough)
- Any screen that requires explanation before it makes sense
- Any feature that is not directly relevant to what they said in discovery

Every additional minute of demo increases the risk of losing the merchant's attention or introducing complexity that creates hesitation.

---

### Closing the Demo (3 minutes)

After the walkthrough, summarise in the merchant's language:

> "What you've seen today is [specific problem from discovery] solved by [specific feature shown]. The setup takes 10 minutes. Your team adds a member in 30 seconds. The programme runs automatically after that."

Then transition to the trial:

> "The best way to know if this is right for your business is to try it with real customers. The 30-day trial is completely free — no credit card, no commitment. If it works for you, great. If not, no harm done. What do you think — would you like to start today?"

---

### Trial Invitation Language

Use these phrasings — they remove friction and reduce perceived risk:

- "Start free today — you can have your first member in by tonight."
- "The trial is 30 days, full access, no card needed. It's the least risky way to evaluate it."
- "I'll walk you through setup if you want — it takes 10 minutes together."
- "You could add your first real customer before you close today. That's the real test."

---

### If They Don't Start Today

If the merchant does not start the trial at the demo, agree on a specific next action and date:

> "Totally understand. When would be a good time to pick this up — would [specific day] work?"

Set a follow-up task in CRM immediately. Do not leave without a defined next step.

---

## 8. Objection Handling

### How to Handle Objections

An objection is not a rejection. An objection is information. It tells you what the merchant is worried about, what they misunderstood, or what they need before they can move forward.

The standard objection response process:

1. **Acknowledge** — show that you heard the objection without dismissing it
2. **Clarify** — make sure you understand what they actually mean
3. **Ask** — use a question to open the conversation rather than launching into a rebuttal
4. **Respond** — address the real concern with a direct, honest answer
5. **Check** — confirm the objection has been addressed before moving forward

---

### Objection 1 — "It's too expensive."

**What they say:** "I'm not sure I can justify the monthly cost right now."

**What they actually mean:** They haven't yet seen enough value to weigh the cost against an outcome. Or they've had a bad experience paying for software that didn't deliver.

**Questions to ask:**
- "What would you need to see from the programme to feel the cost was worth it?"
- "If it brought back one regular a week who'd otherwise not have come in, would that cover the cost?"
- "Have you had a bad experience with software subscriptions before?"

**Suggested response:**
> "I completely understand. That's exactly why the trial is free — no credit card, no commitment. You use it with real customers for 30 days and you decide based on what you actually see, not on what I'm telling you now. The cost question becomes a lot easier to answer when you've seen it work."

**When to stop pushing:**
If the merchant is clear that they cannot afford any additional spend right now and the conversation has confirmed this is a genuine financial constraint — not a value concern — acknowledge it, leave the trial link, and set a follow-up for next quarter.

---

### Objection 2 — "I'm too busy."

**What they say:** "I'd love to but I just don't have time to learn a new system."

**What they actually mean:** They're worried the learning curve will be another item on an already long to-do list. They've probably had experiences with software that promised simplicity and delivered complexity.

**Questions to ask:**
- "What part do you imagine takes the most time?"
- "If setup genuinely took 10 minutes, would that change things?"
- "Would you like me to walk you through it right now — together it takes about 10 minutes."

**Suggested response:**
> "The setup is designed for someone who has two customers waiting. It's six steps. 10 minutes. After that, the two things your staff do are: add a customer when they come in for the first time — 30 seconds — and record a purchase — 15 seconds. That's the entire ongoing time commitment. There's no report to run, no settings to manage, nothing to maintain."

**When to stop pushing:**
If the merchant is genuinely overwhelmed and cannot prioritise even 10 minutes right now, acknowledge it and ask if there's a quieter period coming up — after a holiday, at the start of a new month. Set a specific follow-up date.

---

### Objection 3 — "We already use paper stamp cards."

**What they say:** "Our customers are used to the paper cards — I don't want to confuse them."

**What they actually mean:** They see switching as a risk rather than an improvement. They are underestimating how often customers lose or forget their cards.

**Questions to ask:**
- "How often do customers actually complete the full stamp card?"
- "What happens when a customer loses their card — do you give them credit for the stamps?"
- "Do you know today who has almost completed their card?"

**Suggested response:**
> "The paper card does work — for customers who remember to bring it. But half of them lose it before it's done. And when that happens, you've lost the record. With OneMember, your customer's progress is in the system. They don't need a card — they give you their name. And you know at any moment who's close to their reward and who hasn't been in for a while."

**When to stop pushing:**
If the merchant has a deep attachment to the paper card for cultural or personal reasons and is genuinely not open to changing, don't force it. Say you're always available when they're ready and move on.

---

### Objection 4 — "I need to discuss it with my partner / manager."

**What they say:** "I need to talk to my business partner before I sign up for anything."

**What they actually mean:** This is often a legitimate need. Sometimes it's also a deflection. Treat it as genuine unless you have clear evidence otherwise.

**Questions to ask:**
- "Absolutely — what do you think your partner's main concern would be?"
- "Is there anything I can prepare to help that conversation?"
- "Would it help if I could join a call with both of you at the same time?"

**Suggested response:**
> "Completely understandable. One thing that might help — the trial is free and needs no credit card. Your partner can see exactly what it is before any financial decision is made. Would it help if I sent you a short summary of what we discussed to share with them? Then if they have questions, I'm happy to answer them directly."

**When to stop pushing:**
Never. If a partner or co-decision-maker is involved, that conversation needs to happen. Make it as easy as possible and give them the tools to have it.

---

### Objection 5 — "My customers won't use it / don't want loyalty programmes."

**What they say:** "I tried something similar before and my customers just didn't engage."

**What they actually mean:** A prior loyalty programme or app failed — either because customers had to download something, the programme was confusing, or the merchant didn't use it consistently.

**Questions to ask:**
- "What was the previous programme like — did customers need to download an app?"
- "How did your team promote it at the counter?"
- "How long did you run it before you stopped?"

**Suggested response:**
> "The most common reason loyalty programmes don't work is that customers have to do something — download an app, create an account, carry a card. OneMember puts your team in charge. Customers just give a name. The loyalty happens invisibly in the background. The question isn't whether customers want loyalty — they do. The question is whether the programme is easy enough to use."

**When to stop pushing:**
If a merchant had a genuinely traumatic experience with prior software and is deeply skeptical, don't try to overcome it in one conversation. Acknowledge the experience, say you understand why they're cautious, and invite them to watch rather than participate: "What if you tried it with 5 customers over the next 2 weeks — just to test the experience? No commitment."

---

### Objection 6 — "It's too complicated / technology is not my thing."

**What they say:** "I'm not good with technology."

**What they actually mean:** They have a fear of looking foolish, making mistakes, or being stuck with something they don't understand.

**Questions to ask:**
- "What specifically worries you about it?"
- "Have you ever used any apps on your phone that you found easy?"
- "Can I show you just the member-adding screen — that's the one you'd use most often?"

**Suggested response:**
> "I hear this a lot — and then the merchant adds their first member and says 'that's it?'. It's genuinely simpler than most things you're already using. Let me show you one screen. Just one. If it's still confusing after that, I'll stop."

Then show the Add Member screen only. Do not proceed past it until they express comfort.

**When to stop pushing:**
If you've shown the simplest screen and the merchant is still clearly uncomfortable, don't push further. Leave the trial link. Say: "When you're ready, I'm here to walk through it with you step by step. We can do the setup together in 10 minutes."

---

### Objection 7 — "We're too small for this."

**What they say:** "I only have 20 regular customers — it's not worth it."

**What they actually mean:** They're comparing themselves to a large business with hundreds of members, and they don't see how a small programme delivers value.

**Questions to ask:**
- "What's one of those 20 regulars worth to you over a year?"
- "If a loyalty programme brought back just two people who had drifted away, would that matter?"

**Suggested response:**
> "The businesses that see the most personal impact from OneMember are often the smallest. When you have 20 regulars, each one matters enormously. Knowing their name, their history, their birthday — and giving them a reason to stay — is easier with 20 members than with 2,000. And the programme costs less than one customer visit per day."

**When to stop pushing:**
If the merchant genuinely has a very small business with very low repeat customer volume, acknowledge it honestly: "You're right that the full impact comes with more members. The free trial gives you a chance to see if you can build that list. But if you're not adding at least 20 members in 30 days, it may not be the right moment yet."

---

### Objection 8 — "I'm worried about data privacy."

**What they say:** "I'm not comfortable collecting customer data."

**What they actually mean:** They may have concerns about legal compliance, customer trust, or responsibility for managing personal data.

**Questions to ask:**
- "What specifically concerns you — is it the data collection itself, or what happens to it?"
- "Do you currently collect any customer information — a phone number, a reservation name?"

**Suggested response:**
> "The data you collect in OneMember is exactly what you'd ask for when a customer books or makes a reservation: a name and a phone number. OneMember stores it securely and it's never shared with third parties. You own the data. And in Thailand, customers have become more comfortable with businesses they trust holding their contact details — especially when they're getting something in return."

**When to stop pushing:**
If a merchant has a specific legal concern in their industry (e.g., healthcare), acknowledge it, don't minimise it, and suggest they review the Privacy Policy before starting the trial.

---

### Objection 9 — "I already tried something and it didn't work."

**What they say:** "We had a loyalty app before — it was more work than it was worth."

**What they actually mean:** A prior experience with loyalty software was disappointing. They have low confidence that this will be different.

**Questions to ask:**
- "What was the biggest problem with the previous one?"
- "Was the issue with the setup, the customer experience, or getting staff to use it consistently?"
- "Did customers need to download an app?"

**Suggested response:**
> "That experience is actually very common — most loyalty apps require your customers to do something, and most customers don't bother. OneMember is the opposite: your team manages everything, customers just give their name. That one difference changes everything about adoption. Would you be willing to try 10 days — just to see if the experience is different?"

**When to stop pushing:**
If the merchant's prior experience was painful enough to create a strong emotional barrier, don't force it. Acknowledge the experience fully: "That sounds frustrating. I understand why you'd be cautious." Leave it open and follow up in 30–60 days.

---

### Objection 10 — "What happens to my customer data if I cancel?"

**What they say:** "I don't want to be locked in — what if I need to leave?"

**What they actually mean:** A reasonable concern about data portability and business continuity.

**Questions to ask:**

## 9. Trial Success Playbook

The trial period is the most important sales moment. A merchant who activates — adds members, records purchases, and reaches a first redemption — converts to paid at a significantly higher rate than one who sets up and does nothing else.

Every touchpoint during the trial should move the merchant closer to activation. Contact should be helpful, not sales-driven.

---

### Day 0 — Trial Starts

**Merchant objective:** Complete setup and understand what to do next.
**Sales objective:** Confirm setup is complete; set the Day 3 expectation.

**Suggested contact:** Welcome call or message (same day, within 2–4 hours of sign-up).

> "Welcome — just wanted to check in and make sure the setup went smoothly. Have you had a chance to create your campaign and configure your reward? That's the first step. If you're ready, the next one is adding your first real member — the next time a regular walks in. I'll check back in a couple of days to see how it's going."

**Warning signs:**
- Merchant has not completed the setup wizard after 24 hours — trigger a follow-up
- Merchant signed up but hasn't logged in again — trigger an in-app message and personal outreach

**Recovery action:**
If setup is incomplete, offer a 10-minute setup call: "Let's do it together — it takes 10 minutes and you're done."

---

### Day 3 — First Check-In

**Merchant objective:** Have added at least 1–3 real members.
**Sales objective:** Confirm members have been added; offer guidance if they haven't.

**Suggested contact:** Short message or email.

> "Three days in — how is it going? Have you had a chance to add your first member? If you haven't, the next regular who walks in is the perfect opportunity. Adding them takes 30 seconds: name and phone number. After that, recording their first purchase is 15 seconds. That's the whole first step — let me know how it goes."

**Warning signs:**
- No members added after 3 days
- Merchant says they haven't had time

**Recovery action:**
If no members added: "Let's simplify this. The next customer who comes in that you know by name — that's your first member. You don't need to announce anything special. Just say: 'We have a new loyalty programme — can I add your number?' That's it."

---

### Day 7 — First Week Review

**Merchant objective:** Have added 3–10 members and recorded at least 1 purchase.
**Sales objective:** Celebrate progress; identify any friction; introduce the dashboard picture.

**Suggested contact:** Email or call.

> "A week in — well done for getting started. How many members do you have so far? Have you had a chance to record any purchases? I'd love to hear how it's been going at the counter. Is there anything that's felt awkward or unclear?"

**Warning signs:**
- Members added but no purchases recorded
- Merchant is adding members but not explaining the programme to customers
- Merchant is discouraged because not enough customers have asked about it

**Recovery action:**
If members added but no purchases: "The next step is recording a purchase for one of the members you've already added. Next time they come in — look up their name, enter the purchase amount, and confirm. You'll see the points or stamps update instantly. That's the moment that makes the programme feel real."

---

### Day 14 — Halfway Check-In

**Merchant objective:** Beginning to see a pattern — some members are returning, stamps/points are accumulating.
**Sales objective:** Reinforce value with specific evidence from their own dashboard; prepare the upgrade conversation context.

**Suggested contact:** Email with a summary of their account activity.

> "You're halfway through your trial. Here's what's happened so far: [X members added, X purchases recorded, X rewards on track]. The picture is starting to form. The members who are accumulating points are the ones who are coming back. Is there one of them you've noticed returning more consistently since you added them?"

**Warning signs:**
- Less than 5 members after 14 days
- Merchant is still not recording purchases consistently

**Recovery action:**
If activity is low: "Is there something about the recording process that isn't fitting into the flow at the counter? I'd like to understand what's getting in the way."

---

### Day 21 — Conversion Preparation

**Merchant objective:** Approaching or having reached first redemption — the trial's key value moment.
**Sales objective:** Introduce the plan options; begin the upgrade conversation naturally based on evidence in their account.

**Suggested contact:** Call preferred; email if call is not possible.

> "Nine days left in your trial. Here's what I'm seeing: [specific data from their account]. One of your members is [X stamps / Y points] away from their first reward. That's the moment this really clicks — when a customer comes in and claims something they've been working toward. 

> I wanted to give you a heads up about what happens after Day 30. You can continue on a paid plan — everything stays exactly the same, your members keep their progress — or you move to the free plan automatically. The difference is [specific feature the merchant has been using]. What's your thinking at this stage?"

**Warning signs:**
- Merchant has not reached the dashboard or knows what members are close to a reward
- Merchant is unsure what happens after the trial

**Recovery action:**
Address the unknown: Walk through the plan options clearly and simply. Focus on the specific features they've been using.

---

### Day 30 — Trial End / Conversion Decision

**Merchant objective:** Make a clear, informed decision about continuing.
**Sales objective:** Close the upgrade, or understand clearly why not and set a specific follow-up plan.

**Suggested contact:** Call on Day 28 or 29 — not Day 30 itself.

> "Your trial ends tomorrow. I want to make sure you have everything you need to decide what comes next. Based on what we've seen over 30 days, [summarise their activity]. The plan I'd recommend for where you are now is [specific plan] because [specific reason based on their usage].

> If you're ready to continue, upgrading takes less than a minute. If you're not ready yet, your account moves to the free plan and your members keep all their progress — nothing is lost. What would help you decide?"

**Warning signs:**
- Merchant is unresponsive after Day 25 — escalate outreach
- Merchant expresses dissatisfaction — identify root cause before suggesting upgrade

**Recovery action (if not converting):**
> "Completely understand. Your account stays accessible on the free plan. Can I ask — was there something specific that didn't meet your expectation, or is this more about timing? I want to understand so we can do better."

## 10. Closing Techniques

Closing is not a trick. In consultative selling, closing is the natural end of a well-run process. When a merchant understands the problem, has seen the solution, and believes in the risk-free trial, asking them to start is straightforward.

The techniques below are professional and honest. None of them involve pressure.

---

### Assumptive Close

Use when the merchant has been engaged throughout the demo and has not raised major objections.

> "Let's get you set up today. It takes 10 minutes and your first member can be added before you close tonight."

The assumptive close treats the decision as already made — because the conversation has led naturally there. If the merchant isn't ready, they will say so and the conversation continues.

---

### Summary Close

Use when the merchant has heard a lot of information and may be weighing multiple factors.

> "To recap what we've covered: your main challenge was [X]. What OneMember does is [Y]. Setup takes 10 minutes. The trial is free and needs no credit card. The first 30 days give you real evidence from your own business about whether this works. Does that feel like a reasonable next step?"

The summary close reduces complexity by restating the key points simply, and asks for agreement rather than a purchase decision.

---

### Question Close

Use when the merchant seems interested but hasn't articulated a specific reason to proceed.

> "Based on everything you've seen today, is there any reason not to start the trial?"

This turns the ask around. If there is no reason not to, the natural answer is to start. If there is a reason, it surfaces the real objection and the conversation can address it.

---

### ROI Close

Use when the merchant is financially motivated and has articulated a sense of what customer retention is worth to their business.

> "You mentioned that a regular customer who comes in twice a week is worth roughly [X] per month to you. If OneMember brings back one of those who had drifted — even once every two months — the programme has paid for itself. Is that a reasonable standard to test it against?"

Never invent a ROI figure. Use the merchant's own numbers from discovery. If they haven't mentioned numbers, ask: "What would one returning regular be worth in a typical month?"

---

### Referral Close

Use with a warm referral introduction — where the prospect was referred by someone they trust.

> "[Referrer name] mentioned you were thinking about this — they've been using it for [X weeks] and said [what the referrer said]. You're already halfway there in terms of trust. The trial is the same for you — free, 30 days, no card. Want to give it the same try they did?"

A referral close leverages peer evidence and trust, which is the most powerful buying signal in the SME market.

---

### Trial Extension Guidance

If a merchant is not ready to upgrade at Day 30 but is actively using the product and has expressed genuine interest:

- Offer to discuss a short extension if genuinely warranted by circumstances (e.g., they were travelling for half the trial period and didn't get a fair chance to evaluate)
- Do not offer extensions as a default — it delays the conversion decision and sets a precedent
- An extension should come with a specific milestone: "If we extend to Day 45, the goal is to get your first redemption by Day 40 so you have real evidence to decide on."

---

### When NOT to Close

Do not attempt a close when:

- The merchant has explicitly said they need more time — respect it; set a date to revisit
- The merchant has a genuine objection that has not been resolved — closing over an unresolved objection creates buyer's remorse
- The merchant is clearly distracted or in the middle of a busy period — ask when a better time would be
- The decision-maker is not present — closing with a non-decision-maker wastes everyone's time
- The merchant's business is a poor fit that you've only just recognised — do not close someone who isn't a good fit just to hit a number

## 11. Referral Strategy

Referrals from existing merchants are the highest-quality and lowest-cost source of new merchants. A merchant who recommends OneMember to a peer has already done the qualification, trust-building, and objection-handling. The referred prospect arrives with context and credibility.

---

### How to Ask

The best referral requests are specific, low-pressure, and natural. Do not ask for referrals transactionally.

**In conversation:**
> "Can I ask — do you know anyone else in the [industry] space who you think would benefit from something like this? Even one person. I'd love an introduction if you're comfortable with it."

**In writing:**
> "If you know another business owner who you think would find OneMember useful, an introduction means a lot. You can share my details or your trial link — either works."

The ask should feel like you're doing the prospect a favour, not asking the merchant for something.

---

### When to Ask

The right moment matters.

**Best moments:**
- When the merchant first sees their initial redemption — the emotional high point of early use
- When a merchant says something like "I wish I'd started this sooner"
- When a merchant completes their first month on a paid plan and is happy
- At the end of a helpful support or check-in conversation

**Moments to avoid:**
- The first contact or discovery conversation
- Any moment when the merchant has raised a complaint or unresolved issue
- At the same time as asking them to upgrade — one ask per conversation

---

### Who to Ask

The best referral targets are merchants who:

- Have reached the first reward redemption milestone
- Are active (logging in regularly, adding members consistently)
- Have said something positive about the programme without prompting
- Are well-connected in their industry or business community

---

### Referral Rewards

A referral incentive should exist — account credit, extended trial for the referrer, or a discount month. The specific structure and value are commercial decisions to be determined by the Product Owner before launch. Do not promise specific financial incentives until the programme is formally defined.

What you can say before the programme is defined:
> "We'll make sure you're looked after for any referral that converts — I'll confirm the details once we have the programme finalised."

---

### Partner Opportunities

Beyond individual merchant referrals, structured partnerships create referral pipelines.

**High-value partner categories:**

| Partner Type | Why They Work |
|-------------|--------------|
| **Business associations** | Chamber of commerce networks, SME associations, F&B trade groups — trusted peer communities where word travels fast |
| **POS vendors** | POS systems complement OneMember without competing with it — a natural referral relationship |
| **Marketing agencies serving SMEs** | Agencies that help merchants with social media and advertising can include OneMember as a retention solution |
| **Accountants and bookkeepers** | Trusted financial advisors to small business owners — high-trust referrals |
| **Business consultants** | Consultants helping merchants grow can recommend OneMember as a retention tool |

**Partner outreach principles:**
- Lead with mutual benefit, not commission structures
- Emphasise that OneMember complements (not replaces) the partner's core offering
- Provide easy-to-share materials (one-page summary, trial link, co-branded if appropriate)
- Give partners the information they need to answer basic questions, not to give a full demo

## 13. CRM Guidance

### Lead Stages

Use these stages to track every prospect in the pipeline. Every lead should be in exactly one stage at any time.

| Stage | Definition |
|-------|-----------|
| **New Lead** | Identified; no contact made |
| **Contacted** | First contact made; awaiting response |
| **Qualified** | Qualification complete; criteria met |
| **Discovery Scheduled** | Discovery meeting confirmed |
| **Discovery Complete** | Discovery done; demo pending |
| **Demo Scheduled** | Demo confirmed |
| **Demo Complete** | Demo done; trial pending |
| **Trial Active** | Free trial running |
| **Trial Activated** | First member + first purchase recorded |
| **Trial Converted** | Paid subscription started |
| **Active Merchant** | Paying, using the product |
| **At Risk** | Active but usage has dropped significantly |
| **Churned** | Cancelled or inactive |
| **Disqualified** | Poor fit; not pursuing |

---

### Required Notes per Stage

Every CRM record should include a minimum set of notes at each key stage. These notes enable any team member to pick up a conversation without losing context.

**At Qualification:**
- Business type and size
- Current loyalty approach
- Decision-maker confirmed (Y/N)
- Main pain in merchant's words
- Qualification score (see Section 5)

**After Discovery:**
- Key merchant problems (verbatim if possible)
- Relevant personas from Merchant Personas doc
- Any prior loyalty software experience
- Specific demo talking points selected for this merchant
- Main objection or hesitation raised

**After Demo:**
- Features shown and merchant reaction
- Objections raised and how addressed
- Trial started: Yes / No / Date agreed
- Reason if trial not started

**During Trial:**
- Date of Day 3, 7, 14, 21, 30 contact
- Milestone reached at each touchpoint
- Warning signs observed
- Merchant sentiment (positive / neutral / concerned)

---

### Activity Logging

Log every merchant interaction within 24 hours. The log should be useful to a future colleague reading cold — include what was said, not just what happened.

**Log format:**
- Date and method (call / in person / email / message)
- Who you spoke with
- What was discussed (2–4 sentences)
- What the merchant said (any strong quotes or signals)
- Next action and date

---

### Follow-Up Cadence

| Prospect Stage | Recommended Cadence |
|---------------|-------------------|
| Contacted, awaiting response | Follow up after 3 days, then 7 days, then monthly |
| Post-discovery, pre-demo | Follow up within 24 hours of discovery; again on agreed demo date |
| Post-demo, pre-trial | Follow up within 24 hours; again at agreed trial start date |
| Trial Day 0–30 | Day 0, 3, 7, 14, 21, 28 (see Section 9) |
| Post-trial, not converted | Check in at 30 days, 60 days |
| Active merchant | Monthly check-in minimum; more frequent for first 90 days |
| At-risk merchant | Weekly contact until status is resolved |

---

### Task Management

Every contact should produce a next-action task in the CRM with:
- Task type (call / email / in-person visit)
- Due date
- Priority (high / normal / low)
- One-line note on what the task is for

Never leave a prospect without a next task. A prospect with no next task is a prospect that will be forgotten.

---

### Pipeline Hygiene

Review the full pipeline at least weekly. Ask for each record:

- Is this stage accurate?
- Is the last contact date correct?
- Is there a next task?
- Are the notes sufficient for a colleague to pick this up?
- Is this still a qualified prospect, or should it be disqualified?

A clean pipeline is a reliable pipeline. Inflated pipelines (keeping poor-fit prospects active) distort forecasting and waste time.

---

### Future Automation Opportunities

As the sales volume grows, the following automations will reduce manual effort:

- **Trial start trigger:** Auto-assign Day 3, 7, 14, 21, 28 follow-up tasks when a trial starts
- **Activation trigger:** Auto-notify salesperson when a trial merchant adds their first member
- **At-risk trigger:** Auto-flag merchants who have not logged in for 7+ days during trial
- **Referral tracking:** Auto-link referrals to the referring merchant in the CRM
- **Churn alert:** Auto-notify when an active merchant's usage drops below a defined threshold

## 16. Sales Ethics

### Our Standards

These standards define how OneMember sells — not just what to avoid, but what we actively stand for. They are not policies. They are the foundation of the trust that makes this business work long-term.

---

**Never oversell.**

Do not promise outcomes that OneMember cannot guarantee. Do not exaggerate the speed of results or the certainty of return. A merchant who starts OneMember with realistic expectations becomes a satisfied customer. A merchant who starts with inflated expectations becomes a churn risk and a negative reference.

If a feature is not yet available, say so clearly: "That's not available in the current version — it's on the roadmap for Version 2." If a merchant's situation is a marginal fit, say so: "Based on what you've told me, I want to be honest that the impact may be lower than it would be for a business with more regulars."

---

**Never invent ROI.**

If you cannot quote a specific return from a merchant's real data, do not quote one. Do not reference statistics you cannot verify. Do not say "businesses like yours see X% more repeat visits" unless you have documented evidence for that claim.

You can use hypothetical framing that the merchant controls: "If your average regular is worth [amount they mentioned] per month, and you brought back even one drifting regular per week — what would that be worth?" Let the merchant do the math with their own numbers.

---

**Never pressure merchants.**

There is no acceptable justification for pressuring a merchant into a decision they are not ready to make. Urgency must be real ("your trial ends in 3 days") — not invented ("we're running a limited-time promotion for this week only" when no such promotion exists).

A merchant who feels pressured may convert. They will not refer anyone. They will leave at the first opportunity. They may tell others about the experience. The short-term gain is not worth the long-term cost.

If a merchant needs more time, give it. Follow up helpfully. Let the trial and the product do the work.

---

**Never criticise competitors.**

Do not speak negatively about other loyalty platforms, paper stamp cards, or any product the merchant is currently using. Criticism of competitors is:
- Unprofessional
- Unverifiable — you do not know the full context of their relationship
- Counterproductive — it puts the merchant in the position of defending their existing choice

Instead, ask questions that allow the merchant to identify the limitations themselves: "How often do customers actually complete the paper card?" is more effective than "paper cards don't work."

---

**Always recommend what is best for the merchant.**

If, mid-conversation, you discover that OneMember is not the right solution for this merchant's specific situation, say so. This will happen. It is the right thing to do.

A merchant you turned away honestly — and who you told why — is more likely to come back later when their situation is right, and more likely to refer others, than one who you sold to knowing it wasn't the best fit.

This takes discipline. It also builds a reputation that enterprise sales tactics cannot buy.

---

**Always respect the merchant's time.**

Arrive prepared. Keep demos to the agreed time. Do not follow up more than the situation warrants. Do not send unsolicited materials. When a merchant says "not now," take "not now" at face value and act accordingly.

A salesperson who respects a merchant's time is remembered differently from one who doesn't.

---

### Why Long-Term Trust Is a Competitive Advantage

In the independent merchant market, word travels fast. A single conversation at a chamber of commerce event, a recommendation in a Line group, a comment in a local business Facebook group — these carry more weight than any paid advertisement.

A merchant who trusts OneMember becomes a source of referrals. A merchant who trusts the person who sold them OneMember specifically tells people about that person.

Trust compounds. Pressure does not. The sales approach in this playbook is not just ethical — it is commercially superior to high-pressure tactics over any meaningful time horizon.

The businesses that win long-term in relationship-driven markets like Thailand's independent merchant sector are the ones that are trusted. Everything in this playbook is built toward that goal.

## 17. Continuous Improvement

This playbook is version 1.0. It was written with the best available information at the time of launch. It will be wrong about some things. It will be incomplete about others. The improvement process is how it becomes better.

---

### What to Review Regularly

**Win/loss interviews**

After every significant win (a merchant upgrades unexpectedly quickly) or loss (a well-qualified merchant disengages or churns), conduct a short interview or review. Ask:
- What was the deciding factor?
- What almost went the other way?
- What did we say or do that made the difference?
- What would have changed the outcome?

Document the findings. Update the relevant section of this playbook.

**Sales objections**

Keep a running log of objections heard in the field. Every time a new objection surfaces that is not in Section 8, document it — what was said, what was actually meant, what response worked. When an objection appears three or more times, it is common enough to add to the playbook.

**Merchant feedback during trials**

Day 3, 7, 14, 21, and 30 check-ins generate qualitative feedback. Log it. Look for patterns. If multiple merchants mention the same friction point in setup, or the same moment when the product "clicked," update the Trial Success Playbook accordingly.

**Feature requests**

Merchants will ask for features OneMember doesn't have. Log every request, including how it was raised and what the underlying need was. Pass these to the Product Owner. They inform the product roadmap, and knowing what merchants ask for helps salespeople set expectations correctly and identify near-miss prospects to revisit.

**Conversion analytics**

Review the conversion rates at each stage quarterly:
- Lead → Qualified: What % are qualifying?
- Qualified → Demo: Where are we losing people?
- Demo → Trial: Is the demo converting well?
- Trial → Activated: Are merchants reaching the first redemption?
- Trial → Paid: What is the overall conversion rate?

When a rate drops significantly, investigate the stage before it. The problem is usually in the prior stage, not the one that shows the drop.

**Support tickets**

Support tickets from early-stage merchants reveal onboarding friction that the sales process can address. If merchants consistently ask the same question after signing up, the demo or the trial Day 0 call can address it proactively.

---

### Quarterly Playbook Review

At least once per quarter, review and update this playbook. The review should cover:

1. Are the qualification criteria still accurate for the merchants we're attracting?
2. Are the industry talking points current and relevant?
3. Have any new common objections emerged that need to be added?
4. Are the demo sequence and timing still right based on merchant feedback?
5. Are the email templates performing — are merchants responding to them?
6. Are the trial touchpoints happening on schedule and producing results?
7. Has the product changed in ways that require updating the demo script or feature descriptions?

Each review should produce specific, documented changes to the playbook — not just a confirmation that it was reviewed.
