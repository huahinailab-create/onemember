## Purpose

This document defines who OneMember serves, who it targets first, and who it deliberately does not pursue. Every product feature, marketing campaign, sales conversation, and customer support decision should be grounded in the profiles described here.

OneMember does not sell loyalty software. It sells customer retention. Every merchant in this document has the same underlying need: they want customers to come back more often. The loyalty programme is the mechanism. Repeat business is the outcome they are paying for.

### Persona 1 — Independent Hair Salon

**Name:** Nida's Hair Studio
**Industry:** Hair Salon
**Typical Business Size:** 1 location, 2–4 stylists, 1–2 support staff
**Typical Monthly Customer Volume:** 150–300 visits
**Average Customer Frequency:** Every 4–6 weeks per regular client

**Current Loyalty Method:** Paper stamp cards, or nothing at all. Cards are frequently lost by customers or forgotten at home. There is no record of who the best clients are.

**Digital Maturity:** Low to medium. The owner uses a smartphone, Instagram, and possibly a basic booking app. Not comfortable with software that requires configuration or technical decisions.

**Decision Maker:** The owner-operator. In small salons, this is almost always the person cutting hair. They make decisions quickly when the value is obvious and the price is within reach.

**Business Goals:**
- Fill appointment slots consistently, especially on quieter weekdays
- Build a loyal base of regular clients who return without needing to be reminded
- Reduce dependence on social media posting to attract new walk-ins
- Grow revenue without hiring additional stylists immediately

**Business Frustrations:**
- Clients visit once, have a great experience, and never return
- Paper stamp cards are lost before clients complete them — the reward never happens
- No way to know which clients have not visited in a while
- Heavy reliance on word-of-mouth with no tool to encourage or measure it
- Staff turnover means new staff do not recognise loyal clients

**What Success Looks Like:**
A noticeable increase in repeat bookings. Clients who mention their reward at the counter. A way to see that the 10th visit reward is working — that clients are actually coming back to earn it.

**Buying Motivation:**
The owner has seen a competitor salon offering a digital loyalty programme and is concerned about losing clients. Or a regular client mentioned that another salon had a rewards programme. The purchase decision is reactive to competitive pressure combined with genuine frustration about client retention.

**Common Objections:**
- "I'm too busy to set this up." — Time is the main barrier, not price.
- "My clients don't use apps." — Concern that clients will need to download something.
- "We already give discounts for regulars." — They have an informal loyalty system but no structure around it.

**How OneMember Solves Their Problems:**
- The stamp campaign is a direct digital replacement for paper cards — familiar concept, no cards to lose
- Setup takes 10 minutes; the owner can do it between appointments
- No client app download required — the merchant manages everything from behind the counter
- The member list shows every client and when they last visited, giving the owner visibility they have never had before

**Features They Will Value Most:**
- Stamps campaign (visit-based, not spend-based — matches salon model)
- Member list with last-visit visibility
- Birthday reward (a birthday treat for a regular client builds personal connection)
- Simple member add flow — faster is better when there is a client waiting

**Likely Subscription Tier:** Starter or Professional. Salons with a strong regular base will want unlimited members quickly.

**Likelihood to Refer Other Merchants:** High. Hair salon owners know other salon owners. Word-of-mouth referral in this industry is strong.

**Estimated Customer Lifetime Value Rating:** High. Salons are stable businesses with predictable recurring revenue. Once a loyalty programme is embedded in daily operations, churn is low.

**Acquisition Difficulty:** Medium. The owner is willing to try but needs a simple, fast demonstration. A 10-minute trial with a live demo converts well.

**Support Difficulty:** Low to medium. Initial setup questions are common, but the product is used in a simple, repetitive way. Few edge cases arise after the first week.

---

> **Why this merchant chooses OneMember:**
> The owner has watched clients disappear between visits and known it was a problem for years. Paper stamp cards were the best available option, but they failed constantly. OneMember gives them exactly what they needed — a digital stamp card that cannot get lost, combined with a list of every client and when they last came in. For a merchant whose entire business is built on relationships, that visibility is transformative. The setup takes 10 minutes, costs less than a daily cup of coffee, and solves a problem they have had for their entire career.

### Persona 2 — Beauty & Aesthetic Clinic

**Name:** Glow Skin & Aesthetics
**Industry:** Beauty salon / aesthetic clinic (facials, skin treatments, semi-permanent makeup, lash extensions)
**Typical Business Size:** 1–2 locations, 2–6 therapists or technicians, 1 front-desk staff
**Typical Monthly Customer Volume:** 100–250 treatment sessions
**Average Customer Frequency:** Every 3–8 weeks depending on treatment type

**Current Loyalty Method:** A referral reward given informally ("bring a friend and get 10% off your next visit"). Some salons use a paper stamp card for specific treatments. Nothing tracked systematically.

**Digital Maturity:** Medium. The owner is active on Instagram and TikTok. They may use a booking platform. Comfortable with apps but not with back-end software.

**Decision Maker:** The owner, who is often also a practitioner. Some clinics have a business manager who influences the decision. Either way, the decision is typically made in a single conversation if the product is simple and the trial removes financial risk.

**Business Goals:**
- Build a consistent return base to reduce dependence on social media visibility
- Increase average client visit frequency — move clients from every 8 weeks to every 6
- Create a professional loyalty offering that matches the clinic's premium positioning
- Capture client information to build a proper database

**Business Frustrations:**
- High cost of acquiring new clients through Instagram promotion, without a mechanism to retain them
- Clients complete a course of treatments and then disappear until they see another promotion
- No systematic way to track who the VIP clients are and reward them
- Informal discount-based loyalty erodes margins without building true loyalty

**What Success Looks Like:**
Clients return between treatment courses without waiting for a promotion. The owner can identify their top 20 clients by value. Revenue in the second month of a new client relationship is higher than the first.

**Buying Motivation:**
A client asks whether the clinic has a loyalty programme. The owner is embarrassed to say no. Or the owner has recently spent on social media advertising with uncertain return and wants a more reliable path to revenue.

**Common Objections:**
- "Our clients are premium. A points card feels cheap." — Concern about brand image.
- "We have a bespoke relationship with our clients — a generic programme doesn't fit." — Overestimates the complexity needed.
- "I need something that connects to our booking system." — Integration expectation that may not be realistic at V1.0.

**How OneMember Solves Their Problems:**
- Points-based campaign is appropriate for premium services — high spend per visit accumulates quickly and the reward feels earned, not given away
- The member profile stores the full visit history, making every interaction feel personal
- No app for clients to download — the merchant manages the programme, maintaining the premium feel
- OneMember is not branded as a "stamp card" — it is a member rewards programme

**Features They Will Value Most:**
- Points campaign (spend-based, appropriate for high-value transactions)
- Member profiles with full visit history and notes field
- Birthday reward (personal touch that reinforces the relationship)
- Clean, professional-looking interface — matches the clinic's brand standards

**Likely Subscription Tier:** Professional. These merchants have higher revenue per client and are willing to pay for a complete feature set.

**Likelihood to Refer Other Merchants:** Very high. Aesthetic clinic owners frequently network with each other and with other beauty professionals. A positive experience generates referrals readily.

**Estimated Customer Lifetime Value Rating:** Very high. High subscription tier, low churn once the programme is embedded, strong referral potential.

**Acquisition Difficulty:** Medium. The sale requires positioning OneMember as appropriate for a premium business, not just a café loyalty programme. This is a messaging challenge as much as a price challenge.

**Support Difficulty:** Low. These merchants are detail-oriented and learn the product well after initial setup. Edge cases are rare.

---

> **Why this merchant chooses OneMember:**
> They have been building their reputation on the quality of their treatments and the relationships they develop with clients. But when a client finishes a course of facials, there is nothing holding them to this clinic specifically. OneMember gives them a structured reason for clients to come back — not a discount, but a reward earned through loyalty. The member notes field means every consultation feels informed and personal. The birthday reward is the kind of touch their clientele appreciates. And the 10-minute setup means they can launch without disrupting a single appointment.

### Persona 3 — Coffee Shop

**Name:** The Corner Cup
**Industry:** Coffee shop / café (specialty or general)
**Typical Business Size:** 1 location, 3–8 staff including part-time
**Typical Monthly Customer Volume:** 800–3,000 visits
**Average Customer Frequency:** Daily to weekly for regulars; monthly or less for occasional visitors

**Current Loyalty Method:** Paper stamp cards. This is the most common current method in the market. Clients often have multiple cards from different cafés, creating no real loyalty — just a collection of incomplete cards.

**Digital Maturity:** Medium. The owner uses LINE, Facebook, and Instagram. Likely already using a tablet-based POS. Open to digital tools that fit into an existing workflow.

**Decision Maker:** The owner-operator, or a business-minded manager if the owner has stepped back. Coffee shops are often run by passionate operators who care about the experience they create. The decision is made quickly if the demo is convincing and the price is reasonable.

**Business Goals:**
- Convert occasional visitors into daily or weekly regulars
- Reduce the revenue gap between busy and quiet days
- Build a customer database that gives the business real information about who comes in
- Create a reason for customers to choose this café over the one across the street

**Business Frustrations:**
- The paper stamp card works for a few loyal customers but most cards are never completed
- No way to reach customers who have not visited in a while
- Competitors are starting to use digital loyalty — the paper card feels outdated
- Staff change frequently, and new staff do not know the regulars

**What Success Looks Like:**
Morning regulars visit more consistently. Weekend visitors start appearing on weekday mornings. The owner can look at the member list and recognise the names. Revenue on quiet days begins to recover because loyalty members are motivated to visit more.

**Buying Motivation:**
A regular customer asks "do you have a loyalty app?" The owner sees a nearby café using a digital programme and loses a regular who mentions it. The owner wants to "do something about loyalty" but has not had the time to research options.

**Common Objections:**
- "We already have paper cards." — They are familiar and low-effort.
- "My customers don't want another app." — Accurate concern that needs addressing directly.
- "It's not worth it for a coffee shop." — Underestimates the value of a daily regular.

**How OneMember Solves Their Problems:**
- Direct digital replacement for the stamp card — same concept, no physical cards to print, store, or lose
- No app for the customer — the merchant records everything from behind the counter
- The member list gives the café its first real customer database
- At the volume a café operates, even a modest increase in visit frequency per member creates meaningful revenue

**Features They Will Value Most:**
- Stamps campaign (coffee shops are visit-frequency businesses, not high-spend businesses)
- Fast member lookup and purchase recording — speed at the counter is non-negotiable
- Dashboard showing active members and recent activity
- Simple onboarding — the owner needs to train staff on this tool in under five minutes

**Likely Subscription Tier:** Starter. Cafés operate on tight margins and are price-sensitive. The Starter tier is likely the right fit for most single-location cafés unless they have an unusually large member base.

**Likelihood to Refer Other Merchants:** Very high. Café owners are social and often know other café owners, restaurant owners, and retail businesses. A positive experience travels fast in this community.

**Estimated Customer Lifetime Value Rating:** Medium. Lower subscription tier, but very high volume of referral potential. The café persona is a strong acquisition lever for other industries.

**Acquisition Difficulty:** Low. The purchase decision is fast when the demo is simple and the free trial removes financial risk. The pain is real and the solution is obvious.

**Support Difficulty:** Low. The use case is simple and repetitive. Adding a member and recording a coffee purchase is the same action hundreds of times a month.

---

> **Why this merchant chooses OneMember:**
> The paper stamp card was always a workaround, not a real solution. Half the cards got lost before they were completed. The owner had no way of knowing who their most loyal customers were. OneMember replaces the paper card with something that actually works — and gives the café its first real customer list as a side effect. The fact that customers do not need to download an app is the detail that makes it work in a high-volume counter environment. For the first time, the owner knows who comes in most often and can see the loyalty programme working in real time.

### Persona 4 — Restaurant

**Name:** Khao Hom Thai Kitchen
**Industry:** Restaurant (casual dining, family-style, Thai cuisine)
**Typical Business Size:** 1 location, 8–25 staff
**Typical Monthly Customer Volume:** 600–2,500 dining visits
**Average Customer Frequency:** Weekly to monthly for regulars; occasionally for most

**Current Loyalty Method:** None. Restaurants of this size rarely have a formal loyalty system. Some offer a physical membership card that gives a percentage discount on every visit. Discount-based loyalty is common but erodes margin.

**Digital Maturity:** Medium. The owner likely uses a POS system and manages social media. Finance-minded and practical. More interested in results than technology.

**Decision Maker:** The owner or business partner. In family-run restaurants, multiple family members may be involved in the decision. The owner is usually the financial decision-maker. Decisions are made methodically — they want to understand what they are paying for before committing.

**Business Goals:**
- Increase table occupancy on weekday evenings
- Build a base of regulars who choose the restaurant for family dinners, not just special occasions
- Reduce dependence on food delivery platforms that take margin from every order
- Gather information about the most loyal customers to build stronger relationships

**Business Frustrations:**
- The restaurant fills up on weekends but weekday evenings are inconsistent
- Many customers come for a birthday or special occasion and never return
- Food delivery platforms bring customers, but they belong to the platform, not the restaurant
- No way to differentiate loyal regulars from one-time visitors — everyone gets the same experience

**What Success Looks Like:**
Families who come for a birthday dinner return the following month for a regular dinner. Weekend regulars start coming on quieter weekday evenings. The owner can see on the dashboard that loyalty members are visiting more frequently than non-members.

**Buying Motivation:**
A reduction in weekday covers. A staff member who previously worked somewhere with a digital loyalty programme suggests it. The owner reads an article about customer retention and realises they have no system in place.

**Common Objections:**
- "Our customers come for the food, not the reward." — True, but the reward provides the tie-breaker when two restaurants offer equivalent food quality.
- "Adding members at the table is complicated." — Concern about front-of-house workflow.
- "We're too busy to manage this." — The owner underestimates how little daily management is required.

**How OneMember Solves Their Problems:**
- Points-based campaign appropriate for variable spend sizes — larger group dinners earn more points
- Member registration can happen at the end of the meal, not mid-service
- The dashboard gives the owner a view of loyalty programme activity without daily management
- Birthday rewards create a specific mechanism to turn occasion visitors into regulars

**Features They Will Value Most:**
- Points campaign (spend-based, appropriate for variable dinner bills)
- Birthday reward (restaurants are a natural birthday destination — a birthday reward brings them back)
- Member notes (dietary preferences, regular table preference, family context)
- Reports — restaurants are data-minded; visibility into member visit frequency matters

**Likely Subscription Tier:** Professional. Restaurants with a growing member base need full features, especially birthday rewards and member notes.

**Likelihood to Refer Other Merchants:** Medium. Restaurants are less social with competitors than salons, but owners often have networks in hospitality, F&B supply, and the broader business community.

**Estimated Customer Lifetime Value Rating:** High. Restaurants are stable businesses with predictable loyalty needs. Once the programme is integrated into the front-of-house workflow, it stays.

**Acquisition Difficulty:** Medium to high. The restaurant decision-maker is methodical and needs to see a clear workflow for how loyalty will be managed during service. A live walkthrough is more effective than written materials alone.

**Support Difficulty:** Medium. Workflow questions are common in the first few weeks. Once the team adopts a routine for adding members and recording purchases, support needs reduce significantly.

---

> **Why this merchant chooses OneMember:**
> The owner knows that their regular families are the foundation of the business. But they have had no way to identify, reward, or communicate with those families specifically. OneMember gives them a points programme that rewards every visit and a birthday reward that turns a one-off celebration into an annual tradition. The member notes field means the front-of-house team can greet regulars by name and remember their preferences. For a business built on hospitality, that personal touch is worth more than the subscription cost.

### Persona 5 — Dental Clinic

**Name:** Bright Smile Dental Clinic
**Industry:** Dental clinic (general dentistry, cosmetic dentistry)
**Typical Business Size:** 1 clinic, 1–3 dentists, 1–3 support staff
**Typical Monthly Customer Volume:** 80–200 appointments
**Average Customer Frequency:** Every 6 months for routine checkups; more frequent for treatment courses

**Current Loyalty Method:** None. Dental clinics almost never have loyalty programmes. Retention is built on quality of care and relationship with the dentist, not a reward mechanism.

**Digital Maturity:** Medium to high. Dental practices typically use practice management software. The owner or practice manager is comfortable with structured software and organised workflows.

**Decision Maker:** The practice owner or clinic director. In multi-dentist clinics, the business-minded partner or manager makes the decision. More formal decision process than most other personas — may involve reviewing a proposal or comparing options.

**Business Goals:**
- Reduce the gap between a patient completing a treatment course and their next appointment
- Increase uptake of preventive checkups (patients who come for checkups discover treatments they otherwise would not have known about)
- Build a patient relationship that extends beyond individual treatments
- Create a professional image that makes the clinic feel modern and well-organised

**Business Frustrations:**
- Patients come for a specific treatment and do not return until they have a problem
- No mechanism to reward patients who refer friends and family
- The relationship is built on the dentist, not the clinic — when a dentist leaves, patients follow
- Competition from nearby clinics makes it difficult to differentiate on clinical quality alone

**What Success Looks Like:**
Patients who complete a whitening course book their routine checkup within six months without being chased. New patients mention they heard about the clinic from a friend who told them about the rewards. The clinic has a list of every active patient and when they last attended.

**Buying Motivation:**
A competitor clinic advertises a digital membership programme. The practice manager wants to modernise the patient experience. A new dentist joins the practice and brings energy for improving patient engagement.

**Common Objections:**
- "Patients come for their health, not rewards." — True, but a reward removes the friction of booking the next appointment.
- "We need it to connect to our practice management system." — Integration expectation likely beyond V1.0.
- "Our patients are too old for digital loyalty." — Dental practices serve all ages; the assumption does not hold.

**How OneMember Solves Their Problems:**
- Points earned per appointment give patients a tangible reason to book their next checkup sooner
- Member profiles track every appointment and note clinical preferences — within the bounds of OneMember's non-clinical scope
- No patient app required — the front-desk team manages everything
- Birthday reward for a dental context: a complimentary consultation, a whitening touch-up, or a discount on cosmetic treatment

**Features They Will Value Most:**
- Points campaign (appointment-based or spend-based for treatment costs)
- Member notes (clinical context, patient preferences — non-clinical only)
- Birthday reward
- Professional-looking interface that reflects the clinic's standards

**Likely Subscription Tier:** Professional. Dental clinics are financially stable and willing to pay for a complete feature set that reflects well on the practice.

**Likelihood to Refer Other Merchants:** Medium. Dentists know other dentists but the professional community is conservative about sharing vendor recommendations.

**Estimated Customer Lifetime Value Rating:** High. Stable business, professional use case, low churn once integrated.

**Acquisition Difficulty:** High. Dental practices have longer decision cycles and higher standards for data privacy and professional fit. The sale requires patient, professional engagement.

**Support Difficulty:** Low. These businesses are organised and staff follow procedures. Once trained, they use the product correctly and consistently.

---

> **Why this merchant chooses OneMember:**
> The practice has always relied on the quality of its clinical work to retain patients. But between appointments, there is no touchpoint that connects the clinic to its patients. OneMember creates that connection. A patient who earns points toward a free consultation has a reason to book their six-month checkup instead of putting it off indefinitely. The member list gives the practice manager visibility into who is active and who has lapsed. For a business that competes on professionalism and patient experience, a well-run loyalty programme is a competitive asset.

### Persona 6 — Fitness Gym

**Name:** Iron Body Fitness
**Industry:** Fitness gym (independent, not a franchise)
**Typical Business Size:** 1 location, 3–10 staff including trainers and reception
**Typical Monthly Customer Volume:** 200–800 active members
**Average Customer Frequency:** Multiple times per week for active members; once per week or less for casual members

**Current Loyalty Method:** Member check-ins may be tracked digitally, but there is no reward for consistency. Long-term members may receive an informal benefit, but there is nothing systematic.

**Digital Maturity:** Medium. The owner is likely using gym management software for memberships and check-ins. Comfortable with software that solves a specific problem but unlikely to enjoy complex configuration.

**Decision Maker:** The gym owner or general manager. In some cases, a business partner is involved. These owners are competitive by nature and respond well to clear outcomes and peer comparisons.

**Business Goals:**
- Improve member attendance consistency — a member who comes twice a week is retained; one who comes twice a month cancels
- Reduce month-on-month member churn
- Create a reason for casual members to become consistent members
- Increase revenue from personal training and class upgrades

**Business Frustrations:**
- Members sign up during New Year or post-holiday motivation and then stop attending within 60 days
- No mechanism to reward members who are consistent — all members get the same membership for the same price
- High cost of acquiring new members makes churn expensive
- Gym management software handles attendance but not loyalty or engagement

**What Success Looks Like:**
Members who attend at least three times per week are accumulating rewards they are motivated to earn. The monthly churn rate decreases. The dashboard shows which members are most active, allowing the team to recognise and retain them.

**Buying Motivation:**
A competitor gym introduces a loyalty rewards programme. The owner sees members leaving and asks why — some mention they are not feeling valued. A new general manager joins with experience from a gym that had a retention programme.

**Common Objections:**
- "Our members already pay a subscription — why would we give them more rewards?" — Conflates subscription cost with loyalty investment.
- "We use gym software for this." — Existing gym software tracks attendance but not rewards.
- "Members will just try to game the system." — Overstates the risk.

**How OneMember Solves Their Problems:**
- Points earned per visit creates a direct incentive for consistent attendance — which is the behaviour that retains members
- Rewards can be gym-relevant: a free personal training session, a free class upgrade, branded merchandise
- The member list gives the team visibility into who is active versus lapsing
- Birthday rewards create a personal milestone that members associate with the gym

**Features They Will Value Most:**
- Points campaign (visit-based points are the most natural fit for a gym environment)
- Member list with activity visibility
- Dashboard showing most active members — the gym can proactively recognise its most loyal clients
- Birthday reward

**Likely Subscription Tier:** Professional. Gyms have moderate to high member volumes and need full features.

**Likelihood to Refer Other Merchants:** Medium. Gym owners know other fitness industry operators, but cross-industry referrals are less common.

**Estimated Customer Lifetime Value Rating:** High. Gyms are subscription businesses with predictable revenue. Loyalty programme churn is low once attendance habits are tied to reward accumulation.

**Acquisition Difficulty:** Medium. The pain is clear (member churn) but the existing gym software creates a perceived alternative. The sale requires demonstrating that OneMember does something the gym software does not.

**Support Difficulty:** Medium. Volume of member interactions is higher than most industries, which creates more questions about edge cases in the first month.

---

> **Why this merchant chooses OneMember:**
> The gym already tracks who walks through the door. What it could not do is give members a reason to walk through more often. OneMember converts every gym visit into earned points — and those accumulated points become a reason not to cancel. A member who is 80 points away from a free personal training session is not going to cancel their membership this month. That behavioural nudge, multiplied across hundreds of members, changes the retention economics of the entire business.

**Likely Subscription Tier:** Starter. Pet grooming salons are small businesses with moderate revenue. The Starter tier covers their needs at a price that is easy to justify.

**Likelihood to Refer Other Merchants:** Very high. Pet grooming owners are deeply connected to their local business community, know other salon owners, and enthusiastically recommend tools that work.

**Estimated Customer Lifetime Value Rating:** Medium. Lower tier, but strong referral potential and low churn. Groomers who adopt loyalty rarely abandon it.

**Acquisition Difficulty:** Low. The pain is real and relatable. The paper-card-to-digital-card story resonates immediately. The pet birthday reward creates an emotional connection to the product idea.

**Support Difficulty:** Low. Simple use case, small team, consistent routine. Questions are typically setup-related and resolved once.

---

> **Why this merchant chooses OneMember:**
> Pet owners are some of the most emotionally invested customers in any industry. They care deeply about who handles their animal and they remember the businesses that treat their pet — and them — with personal attention. The birthday reward for the pet is not just a gimmick; it is a genuine expression of care that the client remembers and tells their friends about. OneMember gives the grooming salon the infrastructure to be that kind of business systematically, rather than relying on the owner's memory alone.

### Persona 8 — Car Wash / Car Detailing

**Name:** Shine Auto Detailing
**Industry:** Car wash and car detailing (express wash to premium full detail)
**Typical Business Size:** 1–2 locations, 3–12 staff
**Typical Monthly Customer Volume:** 300–1,500 visits
**Average Customer Frequency:** Weekly for regular wash customers; monthly for detailing clients

**Current Loyalty Method:** Paper stamp cards for regular wash customers. Detailing clients receive no structured loyalty benefit — pricing negotiation or informal repeat discounts are common.

**Digital Maturity:** Medium. The owner manages a physical operation and is focused on throughput and staffing. Software is used practically. Mobile phone is the primary tool for business communication.

**Decision Maker:** The owner or operations manager. In larger car wash businesses, a manager may be delegated to evaluate tools. Decision is made quickly when the product is simple and demonstrates clear ROI.

**Business Goals:**
- Increase wash visit frequency among regular customers
- Move detailing clients from once every 3 months to once every 6 weeks
- Reduce reliance on walk-in traffic alone — build a base of registered, returning customers
- Create a professional customer experience that justifies premium pricing

**Business Frustrations:**
- Walk-in customers visit once and are never seen again — no record of who they are
- Paper stamp cards for the wash are constantly lost or forgotten — the "10th wash free" reward is rarely delivered
- No way to differentiate the business from competing car washes except price
- Staff cannot recognise or prioritise loyal returning customers

## Who OneMember Is Not For

Defining who OneMember does not serve is as important as defining who it does. Pursuing the wrong merchant type wastes acquisition budget, inflates support costs, and risks product scope creep.

### Large Enterprise Chains

**Description:** Businesses with more than 20 locations, enterprise IT infrastructure, a dedicated marketing team, and procurement processes.

**Why not:** Enterprise merchants need features that are outside OneMember's V1.0 scope: custom API integrations, dedicated account management, SLA-backed contracts, SSO, advanced reporting, and complex multi-location management. Enterprise sales cycles are long and require resources OneMember does not have. More importantly, enterprise merchants are already served by Salesforce Loyalty Management, Oracle CrowdTwist, and comparable platforms. OneMember's competitive advantage — simplicity and affordability — is irrelevant to this segment.

### Government Agencies and Public Institutions

**Description:** Schools, hospitals, government departments, municipal services.

**Why not:** Procurement requirements, data sovereignty concerns, tender processes, and approval cycles make government an impractical acquisition target for a self-service SaaS at this stage. The loyalty use case is also typically absent.

### Manufacturing and Production Companies

**Description:** Factories, warehouses, production facilities, B2B manufacturers.

**Why not:** No repeat consumer loyalty cycle. These businesses sell to distributors or corporate buyers on contractual terms. The fundamental use case — keeping end consumers coming back — does not exist in this model.

### Construction and Contracting

**Description:** Building contractors, civil engineering firms, renovation companies.

**Why not:** No recurring consumer relationship. Projects are one-off. Customer loyalty in this industry is relationship and referral-based in ways that a transactional loyalty programme cannot serve.

### Businesses with Almost No Repeat Customers

**Description:** Wedding photographers, funeral services, emigration consultants, visa agencies, one-time event planners.

**Why not:** Loyalty programmes require repeat visits to deliver value. A business where a customer realistically purchases once in a lifetime has no loyalty loop to close. OneMember cannot help these businesses with their core problem.

### Businesses Requiring ERP or CRM Systems

**Description:** Mid-size companies that need inventory management, HR modules, financial reporting, customer relationship pipelines, or sales funnel tools.

**Why not:** OneMember is a loyalty and retention platform, not a general-purpose business management tool. Merchants who arrive expecting CRM functionality, pipeline management, or inventory control will be frustrated. These are not features OneMember will build.

### Businesses Wanting Heavy Customisation Before Using the Product

**Description:** Merchants who present a list of non-standard requirements before signing up — custom reward logic, complex tier structures, API connections to existing systems, white-labelled client interfaces.

**Why not:** Customisation requests of this type indicate a mismatch with OneMember's positioning. The product is designed to work immediately, for most businesses, without modification. A merchant who cannot use the product without building something first is not the right customer for V1.0. These requirements also signal that the merchant is not actually an independent SME — they are a mid-market or enterprise buyer in disguise.

## Buying Triggers

The following situations consistently prompt a merchant to start looking for a loyalty solution. Understanding these triggers allows marketing to reach the right merchant at the right moment.

**Customer numbers are declining or growth has stalled**
A merchant who is seeing fewer regulars month-on-month is actively looking for solutions. The emotional state is one of mild urgency — they know something needs to change.

**Too many one-time customers — not enough regulars**
The merchant can feel that the business is busy without growing. New customers come in but do not return. The owner-operator recognises the pattern but has not had the language or the tool to address it.

**Paper loyalty cards are failing**
Cards are being lost, forgotten, or never completed. The merchant has been running a paper stamp system and it is producing visible results that are worse than expected. They want something more reliable.

**A competitor launched a rewards programme**
The merchant sees or hears about a nearby competitor offering loyalty rewards and feels competitive pressure to respond. This is a high-urgency trigger — the merchant is motivated to act quickly to avoid the perception of being behind.

**Opening a second location**
The second location forces the merchant to think systematically. They can no longer rely on the owner's personal relationships to drive loyalty. They need a system that works regardless of which location the customer visits.

**Hiring new staff**
When new staff join, the owner realises they cannot transfer their knowledge of loyal customers to a new team member. A system that holds the customer record creates continuity that personal knowledge cannot.

**Spending heavily on social media or advertising without retention**
The merchant has been spending money to attract new customers and is starting to question the return. Someone — a friend, an article, an accountant — asks "but how many of those new customers came back?" The answer is: they do not know.

**A customer asks if the business has a loyalty programme**
One of the most common triggers. A customer who asks implies that they would participate — and that they are already thinking about your competitors who might offer one.

**End of a trial period on a competing product**
The merchant was using a loyalty product that was too expensive, too complicated, or has been discontinued. They are actively looking for an alternative. This is a high-intent acquisition moment.

## Product Priority Insights

### Features That Help Almost Everyone

These features are high-priority development investments because they serve the majority of the merchant personas:

**Fast member add and purchase recording**
Every persona in this document needs to add a member and record a purchase quickly, often with a customer waiting. Speed and simplicity at the counter is a universal requirement. Any friction here hurts every merchant.

**Member list with profile and activity history**
Every persona gains something from a structured customer list. The hair salon knows who has not visited in six weeks. The restaurant knows who is approaching a birthday reward. The gym knows who is at risk of cancelling. The list is not a feature — it is the product.

**Birthday reward**
Relevant to every industry without exception. A birthday reward is personal, timely, and creates the kind of surprise that generates word-of-mouth. It is the single highest-ROI feature for merchant satisfaction and member engagement.

**Both stamps and points campaign types**
Salons, cafés, bakeries, and pet groomers are predominantly stamp businesses. Restaurants, clinics, and retail shops are predominantly points businesses. Supporting both means the product is relevant across all ten personas from day one.

**Member notes field**
Every service-based business uses notes differently, but all of them need it. Pet breed and temperament. Dietary restrictions. Table preferences. Skin sensitivity. A notes field costs nothing to build and creates enormous perceived value.

---

### Features That Help Only Niche Industries

These features are lower-priority unless a large portion of the active merchant base belongs to the relevant niche:

**Spend-based tier structures (VIP tiers)**
High value for spas, clinics, and retail. Low relevance to cafés, car washes, and salons. Defer to a version where the merchant base composition justifies the development cost.

**Group or multi-visitor purchases**
Relevant to restaurants (dinner for four earns one member's points). Complex to design and support. Defer until the restaurant segment is large enough to justify.

**Per-product reward rules**
High demand from retail and bakeries. Complex to implement correctly. Defer to a dedicated sprint once the basic points campaign is embedded.

**Pre-order and advance booking integration**
Highly relevant to bakeries and salons, less relevant to others. Requires third-party integration work that is outside V1.0 scope.

---

### Feature Requests That Should Usually Be Declined

**CRM pipeline and sales management**
OneMember is a retention tool, not a CRM. Adding pipeline management would confuse the product's purpose and serve a completely different buyer profile.

**Inventory management**
Requested occasionally by retail merchants. Outside scope entirely. Redirect to dedicated inventory software.

**Payment processing**
OneMember records loyalty events — it does not handle money. Adding payment processing would fundamentally change the risk, regulatory, and technical profile of the product.

**Customer-facing mobile app (V1.0)**
Frequently requested. Legitimate roadmap item. But adding a customer-facing app to V1.0 would double the scope, delay the launch, and introduce support complexity. Reserve for V2.0.

**Complex franchise / multi-brand management**
The moment a merchant wants one loyalty programme for multiple brand names or legal entities, they have outgrown OneMember's V1.0 scope. Acknowledge the need, do not promise the feature.

## Decision Framework

Before building any feature, approving any marketing campaign, or making any product change, apply this test. These questions are the practical application of the Brand Guidelines' core principle: **does this help businesses keep customers coming back?**

### The Five Questions

**1. Does it help our Ideal Customer?**
The ICP is a small, owner-operated business in a service or food industry with 50–500 regular customers. If a feature or campaign serves a meaningfully different type of business, pause and reconsider.

**2. Would at least 20% of merchants benefit?**
Features that serve a narrow segment of the merchant base consume development resources that could serve everyone. If fewer than one in five merchants would use a feature in their first six months, it belongs on a future version roadmap.

**3. Does it help merchants keep customers coming back?**
This is the core question. If the answer is "yes, directly" — build it. If the answer is "yes, indirectly" — scrutinise carefully. If the answer is "not really" — decline.

**4. Will it make OneMember easier to sell?**
A feature that makes the product more complex to explain, more difficult to demonstrate, or more expensive to support without improving the core value proposition may do more harm than good to the acquisition funnel.

**5. Will it keep the product simple?**
Every feature added to the product has a cost — not just in development, but in complexity presented to every merchant who opens the product for the first time. If a feature makes the product harder to understand at a glance, that cost must be weighed against its benefit.

### Applying the Framework

If the answer is **"No"** to three or more of these questions, the feature should be declined or deferred.

If the answer is **"Yes"** to all five, the feature is a strong candidate for the next sprint and should be documented in `docs/08-Product-Decisions.md` before implementation begins.

If the answer is mixed — some yes, some no — the feature may belong in a future version. Log it in `docs/14-Version-2.0-Ideas.md` and revisit when the merchant base and usage data support the investment.
